\ABSTRACT{%
\textbf{Problem definition:} A dispatcher running a fleet against a
planning model must decide, continuously, whether enough has gone
wrong to justify re-optimizing --- a decision current practice makes on
a fixed clock or an untuned threshold, neither with any error-rate
guarantee. \textbf{Methodology/results:} We formalize the decision as a
sequential test of the null hypothesis that the day follows the
planning model's joint law over travel time, demand, dwell time,
accidents, and breakdowns. The test statistic is an e-process, a
nonnegative process whose null expectation never exceeds one; Ville's
inequality bounds the false-alarm probability uniformly over the entire
monitoring horizon, so the dispatcher may inspect it after every event
without inflating the error rate. Our contribution couples the
underlying bets to the routing plan's own economics: each factor's
evidence is weighted by a predictable sensitivity derived from the
plan's fitted continuation costs, concentrating power where a
deviation would flip the optimal recourse decision; we prove this
coupling stays valid and is growth-optimal against the class of
cost-relevant alternatives. Four results connect the trigger to routing
economics: a regret bound for the induced re-optimization policy, a
closed-form significance level in fleet-economics terms, a theorem that
waiting for evidence dominates replanning immediately, and the
growth-optimality result. We validate the monitor, TEMPO, across 73
synthetic instances, a 19-scenario drift grid, a real last-mile
delivery pilot, and ten baselines spanning classical sequential tests,
a 2024 nonparametric e-detector, a 2026 chance-constrained strategy,
and a 2025 reinforcement-learning routing policy. At matched
false-alarm budgets TEMPO detects drift faster and more often than
every baseline on nearly every scenario, and triggering
re-optimization on its alarm, rather than at the first sign of
trouble, lowers fleet cost by six further points.
\textbf{Implications:} Fleet operators gain an anytime-valid,
economics-calibrated answer to when to replan, at no cost beyond what
an optimal-stopping recourse policy already produces.
}%

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Every dispatcher who operates a fleet under a routing plan faces the
same recurring decision, and it is not the decision that the vehicle
routing literature usually studies. The plan itself --- which customer
each vehicle visits, and in what order --- is typically the output of
a careful optimization against a model of travel times, demand, and
service times. Once the fleet is on the road, that model is tested
continuously against reality: a road runs slower than forecast, a
customer's parcel is heavier than its historical average, a delivery
takes longer to sign for, an accident closes a lane, a vehicle breaks
down. None of these events individually justifies tearing up the plan
and re-solving from the vehicles' current positions --- re-optimization
is neither free nor riskless, since it consumes computation, disrupts
drivers already committed to a sequence, and can make things worse if
it acts on noise rather than signal. Yet delaying re-optimization has
its own cost: a plan that no longer matches the world accumulates late
deliveries, capacity breaches, and unnecessary distance with every
additional event it is allowed to govern. The operational question,
then, is not only \emph{how} to re-optimize but \emph{when}, and it is
this second question that the present paper answers.

Two families of answers exist in practice, and both are unsatisfying
on close inspection. The first is periodic: re-solve the routing
problem every $\Delta$ minutes or every $\Delta$ completed stops,
regardless of whether anything has actually gone wrong
\citep{pillac2013review}. Periodic and rolling-horizon re-optimization
is the industrial default, and recent work continues to refine the
re-solve itself --- for instance as a chance-constrained model
predictive control problem \citep{he2026smpc} --- without revisiting
when the re-solve should happen; the schedule is exogenous to the
data. The second family is reactive but ad hoc: replan once cumulative
lateness exceeds a tuned threshold, or once a load gets ``too close''
to capacity. Thresholds tuned this way have no error-rate guarantee,
invite alarm fatigue when set too tight, and miss slow, compounding
drift when set too loose. A third possibility, familiar from the
statistics of sequential monitoring, is to treat the question honestly
as a hypothesis test: classical sequential detectors such as CUSUM
\citep{page1954} and its data-stream descendants
\citep{gama2014survey} are designed exactly for ``has the
data-generating process changed,'' and in principle a dispatcher could
run one per uncertainty factor and replan on the first alarm. The
difficulty is that classical sequential tests control their false-alarm
rate only at a threshold calibrated to the specific monitoring horizon
and null distribution; used off the shelf, their realized false-alarm
rate can exceed the nominal level by a wide margin, exactly because the
guarantee is not anytime-valid --- it does not survive the dispatcher
looking whenever she likes. Bayesian online change-point detection
\citep{adams2007bayesian} sidesteps threshold tuning with a posterior
probability of a regime change, but at the cost of any frequentist
error-rate control at all.

A separate and more recent strand of statistics offers exactly the
property routing dispatch needs: \emph{anytime-valid} sequential
testing built on e-values and e-processes
\citep{shafer2021testing,ramdas2023safi}. An e-process is a
nonnegative stochastic process whose expectation never exceeds one
under the null hypothesis; by Ville's maximal inequality, the
probability that it ever crosses a threshold $1/\alpha$, over an
arbitrarily long and even data-dependent monitoring horizon, is at
most $\alpha$. A dispatcher can therefore watch the process after every
single event --- every leg traversed, every stop serviced --- and
replan the instant it crosses the threshold, with a false-alarm
guarantee that holds regardless of how often or how she decides to
stop looking. This machinery is not new; it is the modern form of
Wald's sequential probability ratio test, understood through the lens
of testing by betting, and a 2024 line of work already generalizes it
to nonparametric change detection through the \emph{e-detector}
framework \citep{shin2024edetectors}. What is new in this paper is not
the statistical technology but its coupling to the economics of the
routing problem it monitors. A generic e-process, built from
statistically natural bets on each uncertainty factor, spends its
statistical power uniformly: it is exactly as alert to a demand
fluctuation that would never come close to breaching a vehicle's
capacity as to one that would. This is wasteful, because the routing
plan itself already encodes, through its fitted continuation costs and
per-stop recourse prices, exactly where a small perturbation would
flip the optimal decision and where it would not. A planning layer
that solves for recourse under an optimal-stopping formulation ---
estimating, by backward induction over historical demand scenarios,
the continuation cost of carrying the current load onward versus the
cheapest available recourse action --- already computes this local
sensitivity as a byproduct of fitting its own value function. The
present paper reads that value function's local sensitivity back into
the design of a monitoring statistic, so that the same fitted model
that tells the fleet \emph{what} to do when capacity is breached also
tells the monitor \emph{where to look}.

We call the resulting monitor TEMPO --- a Tilted E-Martingale Process
for Online re-optimization --- and its four contributions are as
follows.

First, we construct a master e-process over five coupled uncertainty
factors --- travel time (diurnal, weather-modulated, and
congestion-shocked), demand, dwell time, zone-wide accidents, and
vehicle breakdowns --- each contributing a likelihood-ratio bet whose
tilt is scaled, at every event, by a \emph{predictable sensitivity}
computed from the plan's own fitted economics: how close the running
load sits to its capacity-breach boundary, how much of the remaining
route lies inside an affected zone, how much schedule slack remains.
Because these sensitivities are measurable with respect to the
information available strictly before the event they weight, the
tilted process remains a valid e-process with exactly the same
anytime-valid guarantee as the untilted one --- the coupling is free of
statistical cost.

Second, we prove four theorems that are new to the routing literature
even though the statistical machinery underneath them is not: a
cost-regret bound for the re-optimization policy the monitor induces
(Theorem~\ref{thm:regret}); a closed-form expression, derived from that
bound, for the significance level a dispatcher should adopt as a ratio
of fleet economics rather than a statistical convention
(Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar}); a theorem formalizing why waiting for
evidence before rebalancing a fleet's remaining capacity dominates
rebalancing at the first sign of trouble
(Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting}); and a growth-optimality theorem showing
that the sensitivity-tilted bet is, among all predictable betting
strategies with the same statistical budget, the one that minimizes
detection delay against the class of alternatives that actually matter
to the routing cost (Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}).

Third, we separate the question of \emph{when} to replan from the
question of \emph{what} the new plan should be: the trigger is
paired with a pluggable family of mid-route replanners, from
single-vehicle re-sequencing to fleet-wide rebalancing, so that the
statistical layer of the paper composes with any re-optimization
routine a fleet already uses.

Fourth, we validate TEMPO on a scale unusual for this literature: 73
synthetic instances spanning two established vehicle-routing
benchmarks and nineteen real road networks, a grid of 19 stochastic
drift scenarios crossing five uncertainty factors with null,
mild-to-severe, ramped, transient, and compound variants, a pilot on
fifty real last-mile routes from the 2021 Amazon Last-Mile Routing
Research Challenge \citep{merchan2024amazon}, and head-to-head
comparisons against ten baselines including two 2024
e-detector variants \citep{shin2024edetectors}, the periodic-re-solve
cadence of a 2026 chance-constrained strategy \citep{he2026smpc}, and
the rolling-horizon foil against which a 2025 deep-reinforcement-
learning policy for time-dependent stochastic routing was itself
benchmarked \citep{chen2025dgta}. At a matched false-alarm budget the
coupled monitor dominates or ties every baseline on nearly every
scenario in the grid, and a companion cost experiment confirms
Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting} directly: triggering a fleet-wide rebalance
on the monitor's alarm, rather than at the first stop after a
suspected drift, lowers realized day cost by a further six percentage
points because the rebalancer acts on realized loads rather than
priors.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} positions the paper against dynamic vehicle
routing, classical sequential change detection, and anytime-valid
inference. Section~\ref{sec:model} formalizes the multi-factor
stochastic routing day and builds the master e-process.
Section~\ref{sec:tempo} couples the monitor to the plan's economics and
assembles TEMPO. Section~\ref{sec:theory} proves the four economic
theorems and describes the pluggable replanning layer they govern.
Section~\ref{sec:study} reports the computational study, and
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} concludes.

\subsection{Related work}\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Dynamic and stochastic vehicle routing.} Re-optimizing a
routing plan as information arrives is as old a question as the
dynamic vehicle routing literature itself; \citet{pillac2013review}
survey the dominant re-optimization architectures, nearly all of which
re-solve on an exogenous clock --- every $\Delta$ minutes, every
completed stop, or at pre-committed decision epochs --- rather than in
response to accumulated statistical evidence. The more recent survey
of \citet{ouertani2024dvrp}, covering work through early 2023, confirms
that this exogenous-clock pattern has not been displaced by a decade
of subsequent methodological progress: re-solve triggers remain
overwhelmingly periodic, event-count-based, or tied to a new customer
request, with the re-solve \emph{algorithm} itself --- adaptive large
neighborhood search, column generation, learned construction
heuristics --- being the axis of innovation rather than the timing
decision. \citet{he2026smpc} develop a chance-constrained model
predictive control strategy for vehicle routing with correlated
stochastic service times whose re-solve cadence is exactly this
periodic pattern; our periodic baseline reproduces its trigger
mechanism directly, since the time-window MILP it re-solves is outside
this paper's harness. On the learning side, \citet{chen2025dgta} train
a Dynamic Graph Temporal Attention reinforcement-learning policy that
reacts to time-dependent stochastic travel times end-to-end, with no
explicit change-detection layer and no error-rate guarantee; their own
baseline is the rolling-horizon heuristic of \citet{gmira2021rollingopt},
which we also reproduce. \citet{kadyrov2025drl} train a graph-neural-
network policy under proximal policy optimization for the closely
related setting of joint demand and traffic uncertainty, again folding
the re-optimization \emph{decision} into the learned policy implicitly
rather than exposing it as a calibrated, auditable statistic. Across
both the classical operations-research and the more recent
learning-based strands of this literature, none poses re-optimization
timing as a calibrated statistical decision with an explicit
error-rate guarantee, which is precisely the gap the present paper
fills.

\textbf{Classical sequential change detection.} The CUSUM statistic of
\citet{page1954} and its descendants, surveyed for the data-stream
setting by \citet{gama2014survey}, remain the workhorse tools for
deciding when a stochastic process has drifted. Both require a
threshold $h$ calibrated to the specific null distribution and
monitoring horizon; used with a textbook or borrowed constant, their
realized false-alarm probability need not equal, or even bound, the
nominal level, because the guarantee CUSUM actually enjoys is an
average-run-length bound under continuous, unbounded operation rather
than a probability bound over a bounded, data-dependent stopping time.
\citet{adams2007bayesian} replace the frequentist threshold with a
posterior probability of a regime change under a Bayesian online
change-point model, trading the calibration problem for the absence of
any frequentist type-I error control. Section~\ref{sec:study} shows
this is not a technicality: oracle-calibrating CUSUM and
Page--Hinkley to match TEMPO's realized false-alarm rate, the most
generous comparison this literature invites, still leaves both
classical detectors behind on most of the 19-scenario grid.

\textbf{Anytime-valid inference and e-processes.} A test martingale, or
e-process, is a nonnegative supermartingale under the null with
expectation at most one; Ville's inequality \citep{ville1939etude}
bounds the probability that such a process ever exceeds $1/\alpha$ by
$\alpha$, uniformly over an arbitrary, even adaptively chosen, stopping
time. \citet{shafer2021testing} reframes this as ``testing by
betting'': an e-value is the payoff of a bet against the null a
rational bettor could have placed without knowing the outcome, and the
game-theoretic perspective unifies sequential testing, confidence
sequences, and multiple testing under one anytime-valid umbrella,
surveyed by \citet{ramdas2023safi} and, at book length, by
\citet{ramdaswang2025hypothesis}. \citet{waudbysmithramdas2024} develop
adaptive, data-driven betting strategies (GRAPA and its regularized
variant aGRAPA) that learn the growth-optimal tilt online while
remaining predictable and hence valid; TEMPO's adaptive channel bets
are a direct application. \citet{vovkwang2021combining} establish that
mixtures and averages of e-values are themselves e-values, licensing
both the fixed-grid mixture bets of Section~\ref{sec:eprocess} and
TEMPO's dual-regime combination in Section~\ref{sec:tempo}. Closest in
spirit is the e-detector framework of \citet{shin2024edetectors}, which
builds nonparametric change detectors by restarting an e-process at
every candidate change-point and combining restarted copies by a
running sum (Shiryaev--Roberts) or running maximum (CUSUM-like) rule;
we implement both variants using exactly TEMPO's own per-factor bets
with the cost-relevance tilt switched off, isolating general
nonparametric restart machinery from TEMPO's specific coupling to
routing economics (Section~\ref{sec:study}), and distinguish the
e-detector's native guarantee --- an average-run-length bound over an
indefinitely long stream (their Theorem 2.4) --- from the
finite-window false-alarm bound TEMPO's own Ville argument delivers,
which the reproduction in Section~\ref{sec:study} respects through
matched oracle calibration. \citet{koningvanmeer2026} recently show
that anytime-valid, optionally-continued sequential tests can be
\emph{induced} directly from classical fixed-sample tests without an
explicit betting construction; this is a debate about foundations
rather than the guarantee itself, and we adopt the betting formulation
because it makes Section~\ref{sec:tempo}'s coupling transparent ---
Section~\ref{sec:coupling}'s sensitivity tilts are stated most
naturally as a modification of a bet's stake, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}'s growth-optimality argument is native to it.

None of this statistical literature, to our knowledge, couples the
betting strategy's design to the economics of the decision it
triggers. The present paper's contribution is exactly that coupling,
made precise in Sections~\ref{sec:coupling} and~\ref{sec:theory}: the
planning layer's own fitted continuation costs supply the predictable
sensitivity that tells each channel's bet how hard to push, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} shows this choice is growth-optimal against
the class of alternatives that actually move the routing cost, not
merely intuitive.

\section{The stochastic routing day and the master e-process}\label{sec:model}

We take as given a routing plan --- a sequence of stops per vehicle ---
produced by any planning-time optimizer against a demand and travel-
time model; in our computational study this plan is produced by an
adaptive large neighborhood search over the same benchmark instances
described in Section~\ref{sec:study}, but nothing in the construction
below depends on how the plan was built.
The paper's object of study is what happens \emph{after} the plan is
fixed and the fleet departs: a chronological stream of events, each an
independent opportunity to ask whether the plan is still trustworthy.

\subsection{Events and the null hypothesis}

Fix one vehicle's route, indexed by stop $k = 1, \dots, m$. The
execution of the route generates, in chronological order, an event for
each of five factors at each stop or leg: the travel time on the leg
into stop $k$, the number of zone-wide accidents observed while
traversing that leg, an indicator of whether the vehicle breaks down on
that leg, the net demand realized at stop $k$, and the dwell time spent
servicing it. We index the resulting event stream by $n = 1, \dots,
N$, $N = 5m$, and write $\F_{n-1}$ for the $\sigma$-algebra generated by
everything realized strictly before event $n$ --- the vehicle's clock
time, its running net load, the remaining route, and every earlier
realization. All quantities defined ``before'' event $n$ below are by
construction $\F_{n-1}$-measurable; this predictability is the single
technical fact on which every validity result in the paper rests.

The planning model specifies a null law $P_0$ for each factor,
conditional on a small number of exogenous covariates that the monitor
is allowed to see \emph{without} penalty --- the whole point of writing
these laws explicitly is to make sure that a deviation the plan already
anticipated is never mistaken for evidence against it.

\emph{Travel time.} The travel time on the leg into stop $k$, departing
at clock time $t$, is
\begin{align}
T_k &= \tau_k \, m(t) \, w(R) \, \exp(\varepsilon_k), \label{eq:travel}\\
\varepsilon_k &\sim \N\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_T^2, \sigma_T^2\right),
\notag
\end{align}
where $\tau_k$ is the free-flow leg time, $m(\cdot)$ is a known diurnal
multiplier curve (morning and evening peaks), $R \in \{0,1\}$ is an
exogenous rain indicator realized once per day with known probability
and known multiplier $w(1) = w_{\mathrm{rain}} > 1 = w(0)$, and the mean
of $\varepsilon_k$ is chosen so that $\E[\exp(\varepsilon_k)] = 1$: the
multiplicative noise is mean-preserving. Both $m(t)$ and $R$ are
covariates of the conditional null, not targets of the test --- a traffic
jam at the evening peak that the diurnal curve already predicted, or a
day that a forecast correctly called rainy, produces \emph{no} evidence
against $P_0$ under this construction, precisely because the monitor
conditions on them.

\emph{Demand.} The net demand increment at stop $k$ (pickup volume
minus delivery volume, in a simultaneous pickup-and-delivery setting)
is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:demand}
g_k \sim \N(\mu_k, \sigma_{g,k}^2),
\end{equation}
with $(\mu_k, \sigma_{g,k})$ the planning model's stop-level mean and
standard deviation, fitted from the same historical demand scenarios
used to build the route.

\emph{Dwell time.} The service time at stop $k$ depends on the realized
demand magnitude,
\begin{align}
S_k &= a + b\,|g_k| + \eta_k, \qquad S_k \ge 0, \label{eq:dwell}\\
\eta_k &\sim \N(0, \sigma_S^2), \notag
\end{align}
with $(a,b,\sigma_S)$ fitted service-time parameters.

\emph{Accidents.} Zone-wide accidents are observed as a Poisson process
of rate $\lambda_0$ per hour under $P_0$; the count observed while
traversing a leg of realized duration $T_k$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:accident}
n_k \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_0 T_k).
\end{equation}

\emph{Breakdowns.} Each leg carries an independent breakdown indicator
\begin{equation}\label{eq:breakdown}
x_k \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p_0), \qquad p_0 \text{ small}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Change-point drift}

A day is either on-model, generated entirely from the laws above, or it
drifts: at an (unknown to the monitor) clock time $t^\ast$, one or more
factors switch to an alternative law $P_1$. We parameterize drift by a
kind (which factor), a magnitude, an onset time $t^\ast$, and a profile
--- a step onset, a ramp reaching full intensity over a fixed window, or
a transient episode that clears after a fixed duration --- together
with, for travel time specifically, a spatial extent: a congestion
pocket centered in the service area whose radius grows after $t^\ast$,
so only legs whose destination currently lies inside it are slowed.
This gives drift a \emph{where} as well as a \emph{when}, so vehicles
routed far from the affected zone correctly contribute on-model
evidence even while the day, taken as a whole, has drifted.
Table~\ref{tab:scenarios} in Section~\ref{sec:study} catalogs the
resulting 19-scenario grid.

Under drift, each factor's law shifts multiplicatively in its natural
parameter: the travel-time log-mean in Equation~\eqref{eq:travel}
shifts by $\rho(t)\log(\text{magnitude})$, the demand mean in
Equation~\eqref{eq:demand} shifts by $\rho(t)\times\text{magnitude}
\times\sigma_{g,k}$, the dwell mean in Equation~\eqref{eq:dwell}
analogously, the accident rate in Equation~\eqref{eq:accident} is
scaled by a multiplicative factor, and the breakdown probability in
Equation~\eqref{eq:breakdown} likewise, where $\rho(t) \in [0,1]$ is the
drift's intensity profile (zero before $t^\ast$, one under a step
profile, linearly ramping under a ramp profile, and returning to zero
after a transient clears). No structural assumption of the paper
requires drift to be present or of any particular size; Section
\ref{sec:theory} states its regret bound for a general per-event
Kullback--Leibler separation $\KL(P_1\Vert P_0) \ge \KL_{\min} > 0$
between the post-change law and the null.

\subsection{The master e-process}\label{sec:eprocess}

We now build a single statistic that tests, continuously, whether the
event stream of Section~\ref{sec:model} is following $P_0$.

\subsubsection{E-values and e-processes}

\begin{definition}\label{def:evalue}
A random variable $e \ge 0$ measurable with respect to $\F_n$ is an
\emph{e-value} for testing $P_0$ at event $n$ if
$\E_{P_0}[e \mid \F_{n-1}] \le 1$. A process $(E_n)_{n \ge 0}$ with
$E_0 = 1$ is an \emph{e-process} if $E_n = E_{n-1} \cdot e_n$ for a
sequence of e-values $e_n$; equivalently, $(E_n)$ is a nonnegative
supermartingale under $P_0$ with $\E_{P_0}[E_n] \le 1$ for every $n$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Ville's inequality; \citealp{ville1939etude}]\label{lem:ville}
If $(E_n)$ is a nonnegative supermartingale with $E_0 = 1$, then for
every $\alpha \in (0,1)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ville}
\Prob_{P_0}\Bigl(\sup_{n \ge 0} E_n \ge \tfrac{1}{\alpha}\Bigr) \le \alpha.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:ville} is the entire source of the anytime-valid
guarantee this paper relies on: the supremum on the left is taken over
\emph{all} $n$, including a data-dependent stopping rule chosen by the
dispatcher after seeing the data, so a monitor built from an e-process
may be inspected after every single event without the false-alarm
probability exceeding $\alpha$. No analogous statement holds for a
statistic calibrated only at pre-committed checkpoints, which is
exactly the distinction Section~\ref{sec:study} exploits in the
Bonferroni-fixed baseline.

\subsubsection{Per-channel bets}

Each factor of Section~\ref{sec:model} contributes a bet at the event
where it is realized; the four continuous and count factors are
exponential-family mean-shift likelihood-ratio statistics, and the
breakdown factor is a rare-event stake. Write $z_n = (g_n - \mu_n)/
\sigma_{g,n}$, $(\log T_n - \mu_{\log T,n})/\sigma_T$, or
$(S_n - \mu_{S,n})/\sigma_S$ for the standardized residual of whichever
continuous factor is realized at event $n$, so that $z_n \sim \N(0,1)$
under $P_0$ by Equations~\eqref{eq:travel}--\eqref{eq:dwell}. For a
tilt $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gaussianbet}
e^{\mathrm{g}}(\theta; z_n) = \exp\!\left(\theta z_n - \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^2\right)
\end{equation}
satisfies $\E_{P_0}[e^{\mathrm{g}}(\theta; z_n) \mid \F_{n-1}] = 1$ for
any $\F_{n-1}$-measurable $\theta$, since $z_n \mid \F_{n-1} \sim
\N(0,1)$ under $P_0$ and Equation~\eqref{eq:gaussianbet} is exactly the
$\N(\theta,1)$-versus-$\N(0,1)$ likelihood ratio evaluated at $z_n$.
For the accident count $n_k$ with conditional mean $\lambda_0 T_k$
under $P_0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:poissonbet}
e^{\mathrm{p}}(\theta; n_k, \lambda_0 T_k) = \exp\!\bigl(\theta n_k -
\lambda_0 T_k (e^\theta - 1)\bigr)
\end{equation}
is the Poisson tilted likelihood ratio, again conditional mean one for
any predictable $\theta$. For the breakdown indicator $x_k$ with
$\E_{P_0}[x_k] = p_0$, a stake $w \in [0,1]$ gives the rare-event bet
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bernoullibet}
e^{\mathrm{b}}(w; x_k, p_0) = 1 - w + w\,\frac{x_k}{p_0},
\end{equation}
which has conditional mean $1 - w + w\,p_0/p_0 = 1$ for any
predictable $w$, and is chosen small enough that a quiet leg costs only
$\log(1-w) \approx -w$ in log-wealth while a single breakdown, being
$\gg 1/p_0$ times its null probability, crosses any reasonable alarm
threshold on its own --- a hard, near-point-mass event needs no further
statistical refinement to be detected instantly.

Robustness to an unknown tilt magnitude is bought, at zero cost to
validity, by mixing over a fixed grid $\Theta = \{\theta_1, \dots,
\theta_J\}$ with weights $\pi_j \ge 0$, $\sum_j \pi_j = 1$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mixture}
e(z_n) = \sum_{j=1}^J \pi_j\, e^{\mathrm{g}}(\theta_j; z_n).
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[mixtures of e-values are e-values; \citealp{vovkwang2021combining}]\label{lem:mixture}
If $e^{(1)}, \dots, e^{(J)}$ are e-values for the same null with
respect to $\F_{n-1}$, then any convex combination
$\sum_j \pi_j e^{(j)}$ with predictable, nonnegative $\pi_j$ summing to
one is also an e-value.
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:mixture} follows immediately from the linearity of
conditional expectation and Definition~\ref{def:evalue}; it licenses
both the fixed-grid mixture of Equation~\eqref{eq:mixture} and the
dual-regime combination of Section~\ref{sec:tempo}.

\subsubsection{The master process and Proposition~\ref{prop:validity}}

At each event $n$ exactly one factor is realized; write $e_n$ for that
factor's mixture bet, evaluated at $z_n$, $n_k$, or $x_k$ as
appropriate. The master e-process is the running product
\begin{equation}\label{eq:master}
E_n = \prod_{i=1}^n e_i,
\end{equation}
and the monitor alarms at
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alarm}
\tau = \inf\{n : E_n \ge 1/\alpha\}.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[validity of the master process]\label{prop:validity}
For any choice of predictable tilts $\theta_j(n)$ (respectively stakes
$w(n)$) taking values in a fixed grid, the process $(E_n)$ of
Equation~\eqref{eq:master} is a nonnegative supermartingale under $P_0$
with $\E_{P_0}[E_n] \le 1$ for every $n$, and consequently
$\Prob_{P_0}(\exists\, n : E_n \ge 1/\alpha) \le \alpha$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:mixture} each $e_n$ is an e-value with respect to
$\F_{n-1}$ regardless of which factor fires at event $n$, since a
channel that does not fire at $n$ contributes an implicit factor of
one and hence cannot affect the conditional mean. By the tower
property,
\[
\E_{P_0}[E_n \mid \F_{n-1}] = E_{n-1}\, \E_{P_0}[e_n \mid \F_{n-1}] \le
E_{n-1},
\]
so $(E_n)$ is a nonnegative supermartingale with $E_0 = 1$, and
$\E_{P_0}[E_n] \le \E_{P_0}[E_{n-1}] \le \dots \le E_0 = 1$ by
induction. The tail bound is Lemma~\ref{lem:ville} applied to this
process. \Halmos
\end{proof}

The crux of Proposition~\ref{prop:validity}, and the fact that makes
every subsequent refinement of the paper free of statistical cost, is
that the tilt $\theta_j(n)$ or stake $w(n)$ used at event $n$ may
depend on absolutely anything realized strictly before event $n$ ---
including, in particular, a sensitivity computed from the routing
plan's own economics, which is exactly what Section~\ref{sec:coupling}
introduces --- without disturbing the tower-property argument above.
The only requirement is measurability with respect to $\F_{n-1}$; the
tilt may not peek at the very observation it is about to weight.

Figure~\ref{fig:explainer}, panel A, illustrates the resulting process
empirically on sixty simulated null days for one route: every
trajectory stays comfortably below the alarm threshold $1/\alpha$, and
across the full grid of Section~\ref{sec:study} the realized
false-alarm rate never exceeds the nominal $\alpha$, exactly as
Proposition~\ref{prop:validity} predicts.

\section{TEMPO: decision-relevant coupling and construction}\label{sec:tempo}

\subsection{Decision-relevant predictable tilts}\label{sec:coupling}

A master process built from Equation~\eqref{eq:mixture} alone is
statistically sound but economically indiscriminate: it is exactly as
alert to a demand fluctuation that leaves a vehicle's residual capacity
untouched as to one that pushes it to the brink of a breach, and
exactly as alert to a jam on a leg the vehicle will never traverse as
to one directly ahead of it. This section couples the tilt itself to
the routing plan's own economics, so that the monitor's statistical
power is spent where a deviation would actually change the optimal
recourse decision.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity as a predictable weight}

For each factor $k \in \{\text{travel}, \text{demand}, \text{dwell},
\text{accident}, \text{breakdown}\}$ we define a \emph{sensitivity}
$s_k(n) \in [0,1]$, computed entirely from information available at
$\F_{n-1}$, that measures how decision-relevant a unit of drift in
factor $k$ would be at event $n$ given the plan's current state. The
tilt actually applied at event $n$ is then $\theta_j(n) = \theta_j
\cdot s_k(n)$ for each grid point $\theta_j$ --- the grid itself is
unchanged, but its reach is scaled by the plan's own sensitivity. We
derive the general form these weights should take before fixing the
specific closed forms used in the computational study, so that the
closed forms can be judged as approximations to a stated target rather
than as unmotivated definitions.

\emph{What the weight should measure, derived.} Write $D(W) :=
\widehat C(W) - \min(H, R+F)$ for the (signed) distance, in cost units,
between the fitted continuation cost of carrying load $W$ onward and
the cheapest available recourse price; the planning layer's recourse
policy intervenes exactly when $D(W) > 0$, so $\{D = 0\}$ is the
optimal-stopping boundary. A demand realization $g_n$ at event $n$
updates the load to $W_{n-1} + g_n$; to first order in $g_n$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dtaylor}
D(W_{n-1} + g_n) \approx D(W_{n-1}) + \widehat C'(W_{n-1})\, g_n,
\end{equation}
so the recourse decision \emph{flips} at event $n$ --- the event that
makes the drift decision-relevant --- precisely when $|D(W_{n-1})| <
|\widehat C'(W_{n-1})|\,|g_n|$. Since $g_n$ has scale $\sigma_{g,n}$,
the probability of a flip is, to first order, a decreasing function of
the single ratio
\begin{equation}\label{eq:truesens}
r(n) := \frac{D(W_{n-1})}{\widehat C'(W_{n-1})\, \sigma_{g,n}}
\end{equation}
--- large $|r(n)|$ means the load sits far from the boundary relative
to the size of a typical shock, and a unit of drift is very unlikely to
matter; $r(n) \approx 0$ means the load sits within one typical shock
of the boundary, where drift is maximally decision-relevant. This
$r(n)$, or more precisely a bounded monotone transform of it such as
$1 - \min(|r(n)|,1)$, is the \emph{target} sensitivity: it is exactly
the local, first-order-correct measure of decision relevance, and it is
what Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}'s cost-gradient component $c_{\mathrm
{demand}}(n)$ formally denotes.

Equation~\eqref{eq:truesens} requires the fitted derivative $\widehat
C'(W_{n-1})$ at every event, an additional quantity beyond what the
planning layer already reports. We instead use the closed-form proxy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sensdemand}
s_{\mathrm{demand}}(n) = 1 - \min\!\left(\frac{|B - W_{n-1}|}{B}, 1\right),
\end{equation}
which coincides with $r(n)$'s qualitative behavior under two
simplifying conditions --- the stopping boundary lies near the
physical capacity $B$, and the rescaling term is roughly constant
along the route --- stated and discussed in full, along with the
case in which they fail, in the Appendix. Both conditions hold by
construction in the model of Section~\ref{sec:model}, and the proxy
costs the paper nothing in validity regardless: it differs from the
exact $r(n)$ only in how closely it tracks the growth-optimal
direction of Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}, never in the validity
Proposition~\ref{prop:coupledvalidity} certifies for either choice.

\emph{Travel, dwell, and accidents.} An analogous first-order argument
governs the remaining three continuous channels under an additive
lateness-cost model: if each of the $m$ stops carries an (approximately
uniform) lateness penalty and a delay on the leg into stop $k$
propagates additively to every stop still to be served --- the standard
delay-propagation assumption of deterministic scheduling theory --- then
the marginal cost impact of a one-unit travel- or dwell-time shock at
stop $k$ is proportional to the \emph{count} of downstream stops still
exposed to it, giving the closed form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sensrest}
s_{\mathrm{travel}}(n) = s_{\mathrm{dwell}}(n) = s_{\mathrm{accident}}(n)
= \frac{m - k + 1}{m},
\end{equation}
the fraction of the route's $m$ stops still ahead at stop $k$. Equation~\eqref{eq:sensrest} weights every downstream stop equally
regardless of that stop's own economic importance, a limitation of
the same kind as the demand proxy's; the Appendix works through the
specific failure case (a single, tightly windowed, high-value stop
receiving too little weight) and the refinement it calls for.

For the zonal congestion pocket of Section~\ref{sec:model}, the same
fraction is computed only over legs whose destination currently lies
inside the affected zone, so that a fleet-pooled monitor correctly
assigns zero sensitivity to vehicles routed elsewhere.

\emph{Breakdowns.} A breakdown is decision-relevant at every point of
the route by construction, $s_{\mathrm{breakdown}}(n) \equiv 1$; its
statistical treatment is deferred to the rare-event stake of
Equation~\eqref{eq:bernoullibet}.

Every sensitivity above is a function of information realized strictly
before event $n$ and is therefore $\F_{n-1}$-measurable, so it may be
substituted directly for a fixed tilt in Proposition~\ref{prop:validity}
without disturbing its proof --- this measurability property, and only
this property, is what Proposition~\ref{prop:coupledvalidity} certifies;
it is deliberately agnostic to which of the sensitivity functions above,
or their exact-gradient refinements, is used, which is why the
approximations just flagged cost the paper nothing in validity and are
purely a question of statistical power, addressed directly by
Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}.

\begin{proposition}[validity of the coupled process]\label{prop:coupledvalidity}
Proposition~\ref{prop:validity} holds verbatim with $\theta_j(n)$
replaced by $\theta_j \cdot s_k(n)$ for any sensitivity function $s_k :
\F_{n-1} \to [0,1]$, including sensitivities that are themselves fitted
from historical data, provided the fitting uses only information
available before the monitoring day begins (so that $s_k(\cdot)$ is a
fixed, if data-dependent, function rather than one that adapts to the
very day it monitors).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
The proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:validity} uses only that
$\theta_j(n)$ is $\F_{n-1}$-measurable and takes values for which
$\E_{P_0}[e^{\mathrm g}(\theta_j(n) s_k(n); z_n) \mid \F_{n-1}] = 1$;
since $s_k(n) \in [0,1]$ is itself $\F_{n-1}$-measurable and the
mean-one identity for Equation~\eqref{eq:gaussianbet} holds for
\emph{every} real tilt, the product $\theta_j(n) s_k(n)$ satisfies the
same requirement, and the tower-property argument goes through
unchanged. A sensitivity fitted from data collected before the
monitoring day, such as $\widehat C(\cdot)$ above, is a deterministic
function once the day begins and hence trivially predictable within
that day; only fitting the sensitivity function \emph{from the day it
is applied to} would violate predictability, which the paper's
protocol never does. \Halmos
\end{proof}

This is the paper's central design principle, stated once so it can be
invoked without repetition: \emph{any} statistic computed from the
plan's economics --- a continuation cost, a shadow price, a distance
to a constraint boundary --- may be folded into a predictable tilt at
no statistical cost, because validity depends only on measurability,
not on where the tilt comes from. The plan's fitted value function
thus plays, for TEMPO, the role a linear program's dual multipliers
would play in an exact method: both report the local price of a unit
of drift, and both may be read off without corrupting a downstream
guarantee --- feasibility for the LP, anytime validity here. An
equivalent, sometimes more transparent view is a single exponential
tilt of the joint null in the direction of the plan's cost subgradient,
with the per-channel sensitivities of
Equations~\eqref{eq:sensdemand}--\eqref{eq:sensrest} arising as its
components under Equation~\eqref{eq:master}'s channel-wise
independence; Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} makes this precise and shows it
is the growth-optimal choice, not merely a convenient reformulation.

\subsection{Adaptive betting, dual-regime combination, and rare-event separation}\label{sec:tempoconstruct}

The construction of Sections~\ref{sec:eprocess}--\ref{sec:coupling}
already delivers a valid, decision-relevant monitor; this subsection
adds three refinements, each proved to preserve validity, that together
give the monitor used throughout Section~\ref{sec:study} and referred
to there as TEMPO.

\subsubsection{Adaptive betting}

The fixed grid $\Theta$ of Equation~\eqref{eq:mixture} is agnostic to
the size of whatever drift eventually occurs, which costs detection
delay against drift the grid under- or over-shoots. Following the
adaptive betting strategies of \citet{waudbysmithramdas2024}, each
continuous channel additionally bets with a tilt learned online from
its own past standardized residuals: for channel $k$ with residuals
$z_1, \dots, z_{n-1}$ realized before event $n$,
\begin{align}
\widehat\theta_k(n) &= \beta\, \widehat\theta_k(n-1) + (1-\beta)\,
z_{n-1}, \label{eq:ewma}\\
\widehat\theta_k(n) &\in [0, \theta_{\max}], \notag
\end{align}
an exponentially weighted moving average of past residuals, clipped to
a maximum tilt $\theta_{\max}$, and evidently a function of $\F_{n-1}$
alone. The accident channel adapts analogously via the EWMA of realized-
to-expected count ratios, giving a log-ratio tilt. Each channel's final
bet mixes the adaptive tilt with the fixed grid, $50/50$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adaptivebet}
e_k(n) = \tfrac{1}{2}\, e^{\mathrm g}\bigl(\widehat\theta_k(n)\, s_k(n);
z_n\bigr) + \tfrac{1}{2}\, e_k^{\mathrm{grid}}(n),
\end{equation}
where $e_k^{\mathrm{grid}}(n)$ is the fixed-grid mixture of
Equation~\eqref{eq:mixture} (including a zero tilt, which floors the
channel's null bleed at exactly zero log-wealth per quiet event). Both
summands are e-values by Proposition~\ref{prop:coupledvalidity} and
Lemma~\ref{lem:mixture} respectively, so their average is an e-value by
the same lemma; the adaptive bet costs nothing statistically and buys,
empirically, the ablation gap between the flat, untilted process and
full TEMPO documented in Section~\ref{sec:study}
(Table~\ref{tab:detection}'s discussion).

\subsubsection{Dual-regime combination}

Write $E_k(n) = \prod_{i \le n,\, \mathrm{channel}(i) = k} e_k(i)$ for
channel $k$'s own running e-process (with $E_k(n) := E_k(n-1)$ at
events where $k$ does not fire), and consider combining the four
continuous channels' evidence into a single alarm statistic. A running
product $\prod_k E_k(n)$ pools evidence efficiently when drift is
spread across several channels, but pays every quiet channel's null
decay as a tax when drift is concentrated in one; a simple average
$\tfrac{1}{4}\sum_k E_k(n)$ avoids that tax --- an average of e-values
is itself an e-value regardless of how many terms are quiet --- but
dilutes concentrated evidence by a constant factor of (at most) the
number of channels. TEMPO combines both regimes,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dualregime}
C_n = \tfrac{1}{2} \prod_{k} E_k(n) + \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot
\tfrac{1}{4}\sum_{k} E_k(n),
\end{equation}
so that whichever regime the data actually falls into, the combination
never pays more than a factor of two relative to the better of the two
terms.

\begin{proposition}[validity of the dual-regime statistic]\label{prop:dualregime}
$(C_n)$ of Equation~\eqref{eq:dualregime} is a nonnegative
supermartingale under $P_0$ with $\E_{P_0}[C_n] \le 1$, and
$\Prob_{P_0}(\exists\, n : C_n \ge 1/\alpha) \le \alpha$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
See the Appendix.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[what this proof does not need, and what it does not
claim]\label{rem:independence}
See the Appendix for the discussion of what this validity
guarantee does, and does not, assume about real-world channel
correlation.
\end{remark}





\subsubsection{Rare-event separation}

The breakdown channel's stake $w$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:bernoullibet}
is calibrated so that a \emph{single} breakdown crosses the alarm
threshold on its own; folding it into the product of
Equation~\eqref{eq:dualregime} would tax every quiet leg of every
channel by that same stake, which becomes severe when a fleet of
several vehicles pools its legs into one monitor. TEMPO instead runs
the breakdown channel as a parallel e-process $E_{\mathrm{brk}}(n)$,
splits the significance level $\alpha$ into $\alpha/2$ for $C_n$ and
$\alpha/2$ for $E_{\mathrm{brk}}(n)$, and alarms when either crosses its
own threshold:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tempoalarm}
\tau = \inf\Bigl\{n :\ C_n \ge \tfrac{2}{\alpha} \ \text{or}\
E_{\mathrm{brk}}(n) \ge \tfrac{2}{\alpha}\Bigr\}.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[validity of the split monitor]\label{prop:split}
$\Prob_{P_0}(\tau < \infty) \le \alpha$ for $\tau$ defined in
Equation~\eqref{eq:tempoalarm}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:dualregime}, $\Prob_{P_0}(\exists\, n : C_n
\ge 2/\alpha) \le \alpha/2$. By Proposition~\ref{prop:validity} applied
to the breakdown channel alone, $E_{\mathrm{brk}}(n)$ is itself an
e-process, so $\Prob_{P_0}(\exists\, n : E_{\mathrm{brk}}(n) \ge
2/\alpha) \le \alpha/2$. A union bound over the two events gives
$\Prob_{P_0}(\tau < \infty) \le \alpha/2 + \alpha/2 = \alpha$. \Halmos
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Reset on alarm}

When $\tau$ fires, the dispatcher invokes a replanner (any member of
the family in Section~\ref{sec:decision}), the new plan becomes the
reference against which every sensitivity and null law of
Sections~\ref{sec:model}--\ref{sec:coupling} is recomputed, and every
channel's log-evidence resets to zero. We prove the resulting
per-segment guarantee directly rather than by appeal to segment
independence, because the segments are \emph{not} independent in
general --- the replanner changes the state the next segment starts
from, so later segments' statistics depend on earlier ones in whatever
way the replanner induces. The argument below needs none of that
independence.

\begin{proposition}[segment-wise validity under reset]\label{prop:segments}
Let $0 = \tau_0 < \tau_1 < \tau_2 < \dots$ be the successive alarm
(reset) times on a fully on-model ($P_0$) day, and for $i \ge 1$ let
$A_i$ denote the event that segment $i$ (the interval $(\tau_{i-1},
\tau_i]$) ends in an alarm. Then $\Prob_{P_0}(A_i) \le \alpha$ for
every $i$, regardless of any dependence of segment $i$'s statistics on
the realized history of segments $1, \dots, i-1$; consequently, for any
fixed number of candidate segments $R$, the expected number of alarms
among the first $R$ segments of a null day is at most $R\alpha$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
See the Appendix.
\end{proof}



Algorithm~\ref{alg:tempo} summarizes the resulting monitor as
implemented.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{TEMPO monitor (one segment)}\label{alg:tempo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{TempoSegment}{$\alpha$, plan}
  \State $\widehat\theta_k \gets 0$ for every continuous channel $k$;
         $E_k \gets 1$; $E_{\mathrm{brk}} \gets 1$
  \For{each event $n$ in chronological order}
    \State compute sensitivity $s_k(n)$ from the plan's current state
           (Equations~\eqref{eq:sensdemand}--\eqref{eq:sensrest})
    \If{event $n$ is a breakdown}
      \State $E_{\mathrm{brk}} \gets E_{\mathrm{brk}} \cdot
             e^{\mathrm b}(w; x_n, p_0)$ \Comment{stake $w$ calibrated to $\alpha/2$}
    \Else
      \State $e_k(n) \gets$ Equation~\eqref{eq:adaptivebet} using
             $\widehat\theta_k(n)$ and $s_k(n)$
      \State $E_k \gets E_k \cdot e_k(n)$; update $\widehat\theta_k$
             via Equation~\eqref{eq:ewma}
    \EndIf
    \State $C_n \gets \tfrac{1}{2}\prod_k E_k + \tfrac{1}{8}\sum_k E_k$
    \If{$C_n \ge 2/\alpha$ \textbf{or} $E_{\mathrm{brk}} \ge 2/\alpha$}
      \State \textbf{alarm}; invoke a replanner (Section~\ref{sec:decision}); \textbf{return}
    \EndIf
  \EndFor
  \State \textbf{return} no alarm
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{The economics of the trigger}\label{sec:theory}

Sections~\ref{sec:eprocess}--\ref{sec:tempo} establish that TEMPO is a
statistically valid, decision-relevant monitor. This section asks the
question a dispatcher actually cares about: what does the monitor's
significance level $\alpha$ cost and buy in units of fleet economics,
rather than in units of type-I error? We answer with four results.
Theorem~\ref{thm:regret} bounds the regret, in realized cost, of
re-optimizing on TEMPO's alarm rather than at the (unobservable) true
change-point. Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar} inverts that bound into a
closed-form recommendation for $\alpha$ itself. Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting}
shows that this is not merely a statistical convenience: for a
state-dependent replanner, waiting for evidence strictly dominates
replanning immediately. Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} closes the loop back
to Section~\ref{sec:coupling}, proving that the sensitivity-tilted bet
is the growth-optimal choice against the class of alternatives that
matter to the routing cost, and not merely a reasonable heuristic.

\textbf{What is new here, stated plainly.} Every individual
probabilistic tool this section invokes is classical; what is genuinely
new is the \emph{composition} into a bound on \emph{realized routing
cost} rather than sample size or type-I/II error, and the closed-form
economic quantities --- a significance level, a waiting time, a
growth-optimal tilt --- that composition delivers. The Appendix
discusses, tool by tool, exactly which parts of this section are
routine and which are not.

\subsection{Setup}

One operating day produces events $n = 1, \dots, N$ with observations
$Z_n \sim P_0$ up to an unknown change-point $\nu$ and $Z_n \sim P_1$
after it, where $\KL(P_1 \Vert P_0) \ge \KL_{\min} > 0$ per event. TEMPO
runs an e-process $E_n$ with alarm time $\tau = \inf\{n : E_n \ge
1/\alpha\}$ and, on alarm, invokes a fixed replanner $\Rmax$ (any member
of the family in Section~\ref{sec:decision}; the results below are
agnostic to which). We compare TEMPO's realized day cost to an oracle
policy that invokes the identical $\Rmax$ exactly at $\nu$, and write:

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:kl}
Conditional on the filtration, events after $\nu$ satisfy
$\KL(P_1 \Vert P_0) \ge \KL_{\min} > 0$ per event.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:staleness}
The expected extra cost of executing one more event under the stale,
pre-$\Rmax$ plan rather than the $\Rmax$-replanned plan, once the world
is in the $P_1$ regime, is bounded by a constant $\Delta_c \ge 0$ (the
\emph{per-event staleness cost}).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:growth}
The e-process's log-increments achieve expected log-growth rate $g > 0$
per post-change event under $P_1$: for the fixed-grid mixture bet,
$g = \max_j \E_{P_1}[\log e^{\mathrm g}(\theta_j; z_n)] > 0$; for the
adaptive bet of Equation~\eqref{eq:adaptivebet}, $g \to \KL(P_1\Vert
P_0)$ by the consistency of the aGRAPA-style estimator
\citep{waudbysmithramdas2024}.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}[what Assumption~\ref{ass:growth} does and does not
buy]\label{rem:growth}
$g > 0$ is the minimal identifiability condition any test of $P_0$
against $P_1$ must satisfy, not a technical convenience; for the
adaptive bet it is a stated consistency property of the aGRAPA
estimator \citep{waudbysmithramdas2024}, not an assumption this paper
introduces. The Appendix discusses what this does and does not
certify, including the information-limited regime it predicts and
which Table~\ref{tab:detection}'s mild-accident row exhibits.
\end{remark}

We also record $C_{\mathrm{fr}} > 0$, the expected cost of one
unnecessary invocation of $\Rmax$ on an on-model day (the detour or
coordination cost $\Rmax$ induces when nothing was actually wrong).

\subsection{Theorem 1: the regret of the TEMPO-triggered replan}

\begin{theorem}[cost-regret of the triggered replan]\label{thm:regret}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:kl}--\ref{ass:growth}, TEMPO's expected day
cost exceeds the oracle's by at most
\begin{equation}\label{eq:regret}
\mathrm{Regret}(\alpha) \ \le\ \frac{\Delta_c\bigl(\log(1/\alpha) +
\kappa\bigr)}{g} \ +\ \alpha\, C_{\mathrm{fr}},
\end{equation}
where $\kappa \ge 0$ bounds the expected one-event log-overshoot of the
mixture bet past its threshold, finite because the tilts are bounded.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
See the Appendix for the full proof via
Wald's identity and the overshoot bounds of
\citet{lorden1970excess} and \citet{woodroofe1982nonlinear}.
\end{proof}



Every individual step of this argument --- the telescoping decomposition,
the Wald-type delay bound, and Ville's tail bound on the false-alarm
event --- is standard in sequential analysis. What is new is their
composition into a bound on \emph{realized routing cost}, which is the
object a dispatcher, as opposed to a statistician, actually wants
controlled.

\textbf{Corollary 1: the optimal evidence level.}

\begin{corollary}[the price of evidence]\label{cor:alphastar}
$\mathrm{Regret}(\alpha)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:regret} is convex in
$\alpha$ on $(0,1]$ with a unique interior minimizer
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alphastar}
\alpha^\ast \ =\ \min\!\left(1,\ \frac{\Delta_c}{g\, C_{\mathrm{fr}}}\right).
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
Write $A = \Delta_c/g$ and $B = \Delta_c \kappa / g$, both constants
with respect to $\alpha$, so that
$\mathrm{Regret}(\alpha) = A\log(1/\alpha) + B + \alpha\, C_{\mathrm{fr}}$.
Then
\begin{align*}
\frac{d}{d\alpha}\mathrm{Regret}(\alpha) &= -\frac{A}{\alpha} +
C_{\mathrm{fr}}, \\
\frac{d^2}{d\alpha^2}\mathrm{Regret}(\alpha) &= \frac{A}{\alpha^2} > 0,
\end{align*}
so $\mathrm{Regret}$ is strictly convex on $(0,1]$. Setting the first
derivative to zero gives the unconstrained stationary point $\alpha =
A/C_{\mathrm{fr}} = \Delta_c/(g\,C_{\mathrm{fr}})$, which is the global
minimizer by convexity; clipping to the feasible range $(0,1]$ gives
Equation~\eqref{eq:alphastar}. \Halmos
\end{proof}

The optimization itself is two lines of undergraduate calculus, and we
do not present it as a technical contribution; the argument in
Theorem~\ref{thm:regret} that produces $\mathrm{Regret}(\alpha)$'s
particular functional form is where the work lies. What Corollary
\ref{cor:alphastar} contributes is the translation of that form into a
decision rule stated in quantities a fleet already measures --- a
per-event staleness cost, an empirical detection rate, and an
unnecessary-replan cost --- in place of a significance level chosen by
disciplinary convention; the same closed form appears throughout
economics and operations research whenever a convex trade-off between a
logarithmic and a linear term is optimized (the classical
economic-order-quantity formula has the same two-line derivation and
the same practical status), and we make no larger claim for
Equation~\eqref{eq:alphastar} than that.

Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar} recasts $\alpha$ from a statistical
convention into a price: the ratio of the per-event cost of staying
stale to the product of the monitor's evidence rate and the cost of an
unnecessary replan. Cheap replans --- a software-only re-sequencing
with no driver coordination cost, small $C_{\mathrm{fr}}$ --- push
$\alpha^\ast$ toward one: replan liberally, without waiting for strong
evidence. Expensive replans --- those requiring driver coordination or
standby-vehicle dispatch, large $C_{\mathrm{fr}}$ --- push
$\alpha^\ast$ down: demand strong evidence before disrupting the plan.
No analogous closed-form recommendation exists in the rolling-horizon
literature, where the re-optimization epoch is exogenous to the day's
realized economics by construction. Section~\ref{sec:study} confirms
the U-shaped trade-off Equation~\eqref{eq:alphastar} predicts on an
empirical significance-level sweep (Table~\ref{tab:alphasweep}).

\subsection{Theorem 2: the value of waiting for evidence}

Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar} shows that a dispatcher who fixes
$\Delta_c$, $g$, and $C_{\mathrm{fr}}$ should not, in general, alarm as
early as possible. We now show this conclusion strengthens for a
\emph{state-dependent} replanner --- one whose output depends on
realized loads and slacks, such as the fleet-wide rebalancer of
Section~\ref{sec:decision} --- because delaying the replan does not
merely trade detection delay for statistical safety; it lets the
replanner act on more accurate information.

\emph{Stylized model.} Consider two vehicles $A, B$ with capacities
$Q_A, Q_B$, current loads $W_A, W_B$, and residual slacks $Y_A = Q_A -
W_A$, $Y_B = Q_B - W_B$, each with $n_A, n_B$ remaining customers ($n =
n_A + n_B$) drawn from a shared, homogeneous pool whose net demand
increments are i.i.d.\ $\N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ after a change-point that has
already occurred, with $\mu$ unknown to the rebalancer. At event index
$s$, having observed $s$ realized demands since the change with sample
mean $\widehat\mu(s)$, a rebalancer reassigns the $n - s$ not-yet-served
customers between $A$ and $B$ to equalize expected end-of-route loads
against slacks, using $\widehat\mu(s)$ as a plug-in for the unknown
$\mu$. The population-optimal split, were $\mu$ known, is the fraction
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optsplit}
\rho^\ast(\mu) = \frac12 + \frac{Y_A - Y_B}{2(n-s)\mu}
\end{equation}
of the remaining pool assigned to $A$; because the rebalancer instead
uses $\widehat\mu(s) = \mu + O_p(\sigma/\sqrt s)$, its achieved split
$\widehat\rho(s) := \rho^\ast(\widehat\mu(s))$ deviates from
Equation~\eqref{eq:optsplit} by the same order. A second-order Taylor
expansion of the expected combined breach cost around the true
optimum, combined with the sampling variance of the mean estimator
$\widehat\mu(s)$, translates this estimation error into an expected
excess cost (the complete derivation is given in the Appendix)
\begin{align}
V(s) &:= \E\bigl[\Phi(\widehat\rho(s))\bigr] - \Phi(\rho^\ast(\mu))
\ \approx\ \frac{\kappa_0\, \sigma^2}{s}, \label{eq:vs}
\end{align}
an explicit, computable constant $\kappa_0$ given the breach-cost
curvature and the closed-form split sensitivity $(\rho^\ast)'(\mu)$;
both are, in principle, available from the same fleet-slack data
$(Y_A, Y_B, n-s)$ that defines $\rho^\ast$ itself, though we do not fit
$\Phi$ explicitly in the computational study and instead treat
$\kappa_0$ as a free positive constant, exactly as $\Delta_c$ and $g$
are treated in Theorem~\ref{thm:regret}. $V(s)$ is strictly decreasing
in $s$, formalizing the intuition that information about $\mu$
accrues, and the value of a plug-in estimate improves, the longer the
rebalancer waits. We emphasize what this derivation does and does not
establish: it is a local (small-estimation-error), single-parameter
(scalar $\mu$) approximation for a two-vehicle, single-rebalancing-
decision model: a real fleet rebalancer solves a combinatorial
reinsertion problem over many customers and many routes
(Section~\ref{sec:decision}), and Equation~\eqref{eq:vs} is offered as
the simplest model in which the qualitative mechanism --- more
realized information lowers the expected cost of a state-dependent
decision --- can be made exact, not as a claim that Equation
\eqref{eq:vs} literally governs the reinsertion heuristic used in
Section~\ref{sec:study}.

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:waiting}
(B1) The rebalancer's plug-in excess cost obeys Equation~\eqref{eq:vs}
for $s$ at or after the change-point $\nu$; before saturation this is
the dominant cost of rebalancing early. (B2) Staleness accrues linearly
after $\nu$ at rate $\Delta_c$ per event, as in
Assumption~\ref{ass:staleness}.
\end{assumption}

\begin{theorem}[the value of waiting]\label{thm:waiting}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:waiting}, the expected day cost of invoking
the rebalancer at event $s \ge \nu$ has the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:js}
J(s) = J^\ast + \frac{\kappa_0\sigma^2}{s} + \Delta_c\,(s - \nu),
\end{equation}
which is minimized at the interior point
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sstar}
s^\ast = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa_0\sigma^2}{\Delta_c}},
\end{equation}
so that invoking the rebalancer immediately at $s = \nu$ is strictly
dominated by waiting until $s^\ast$ whenever $s^\ast > \nu$, i.e.\
whenever $\kappa_0\sigma^2/\nu^2 > \Delta_c$: the marginal reduction in
plug-in error from one more event of waiting exceeds the marginal
staleness cost of that same event.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
Differentiate Equation~\eqref{eq:js} in $s$ for $s \ge \nu$:
\[
\frac{dJ}{ds} = -\frac{\kappa_0\sigma^2}{s^2} + \Delta_c,
\]
which is negative for $s < \sqrt{\kappa_0\sigma^2/\Delta_c}$ and
positive beyond it, so $J$ is minimized at $s^\ast =
\sqrt{\kappa_0\sigma^2/\Delta_c}$, giving
Equation~\eqref{eq:sstar}; the second derivative
$2\kappa_0\sigma^2/s^3 > 0$ confirms this is a minimum. At $s = \nu$,
$dJ/ds = -\kappa_0\sigma^2/\nu^2 + \Delta_c$, which is strictly negative
--- so waiting past $\nu$ strictly lowers expected cost --- exactly when
$\kappa_0\sigma^2/\nu^2 > \Delta_c$, i.e.\ $s^\ast > \nu$. \Halmos
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting} formalizes a conclusion with no analogue we
are aware of in the dynamic-routing literature, where more frequent
re-optimization is typically assumed weakly better because information
is treated as free and replans as costless: replanning immediately
after a suspected drift is not merely statistically premature, it is
\emph{economically} suboptimal whenever the rebalancer's plug-in
estimate has not yet had time to accrue enough information to be
trustworthy. TEMPO's alarm fires only once accumulated evidence ---
and hence, in this stylized model, the realized sample size available
to a state-dependent replanner invoked at the alarm --- is already
large, so it lands naturally near the interior optimum $s^\ast$ rather
than at the boundary $s = \nu$. Section~\ref{sec:study} measures this
directly: triggering the fleet-wide rebalancer of
Section~\ref{sec:decision} on TEMPO's alarm, rather than at the first
stop after a suspected drift, realizes a further six-percentage-point
cost saving (Table~\ref{tab:replan}).

\subsection{Theorem 3: decision-relevant betting is growth-optimal}

The final theorem returns to Section~\ref{sec:coupling}'s design choice
and shows it is not merely intuitive but optimal in a precise
minimax sense.

Consider $K$ exponential-family channels, each with log-moment-
generating function $\psi_k$ (for the Gaussian channels of
Section~\ref{sec:eprocess}, $\psi_k(\theta) = \theta^2/2$; for the
Poisson channel, $\psi_k(\theta) = \lambda_0 T (e^\theta - 1)$), so that
a tilt $\theta_k$ achieves expected log-growth $\theta_k \eta_k -
\psi_k(\theta_k)$ against a true alternative mean-shift $\eta_k$ in
channel $k$. Let $c = (c_1, \dots, c_K)$ be the plan's local cost
gradient --- the marginal increase in expected routing cost per unit
mean-shift in each channel, evaluated at the plan's current state, of
which the sensitivities $s_k(n)$ of Equations
\eqref{eq:sensdemand}--\eqref{eq:sensrest} are proxies --- and define
the cost-relevant alternative class
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pdelta}
\mathcal P_\Delta = \bigl\{\eta \in \mathbb R^K :\ c^\top \eta = \Delta
\bigr\},
\end{equation}
the hyperplane of joint drift directions whose first-order impact on
routing cost equals a fixed threshold $\Delta > 0$.

$\mathcal P_\Delta$ is a modeling choice, not a derived object: it is
the routing-cost analogue of the contamination and shrinking
neighborhoods long used to define worst-case performance in robust
statistics \citep{huber1964robust}, chosen to formalize
``alternatives with a specified linearized cost impact'' in the
simplest possible way rather than as a claim that every real drift
lies exactly on this hyperplane. The Appendix discusses
this framing choice, and its scope, further.



\begin{theorem}[growth-optimality of the decision-relevant tilt]\label{thm:growth}
Among predictable joint tilts $\theta \in \mathbb R^K$, the worst-case
expected log-growth over $\mathcal P_\Delta$,
\[
\gamma(\theta) = \inf_{\eta \in \mathcal P_\Delta} \left[\sum_{k=1}^K
\bigl(\theta_k \eta_k - \psi_k(\theta_k)\bigr)\right],
\]
is finite only for $\theta$ parallel to $c$, and among those,
$\gamma(\theta)$ is maximized at $\theta^\ast = \lambda^\ast c$ where
$\lambda^\ast$ solves $c^\top \nabla\psi(\lambda^\ast c) = \Delta$; for
the Gaussian channels of Section~\ref{sec:eprocess}, this gives the
closed form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:thetastar}
\theta_k^\ast = \frac{\Delta}{\lVert c \rVert^2}\, c_k,
\end{equation}
a tilt whose per-channel weights are exactly proportional to the
plan's local cost-sensitivity components --- TEMPO's construction in
Section~\ref{sec:coupling}, with $c_k$ played by the sensitivity
$s_k(n)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
See the Appendix.
\end{proof}

The proof identifies why a tilt not aligned with the plan's cost
gradient is dominated: any component of the bet orthogonal to $c$ can
be exploited by an adversarial alternative that moves along that
component while leaving its cost impact, and hence its membership in
$\mathcal P_\Delta$, unchanged, driving the bet's worst-case growth to
$-\infty$; only a tilt parallel to $c$ has a worst case that does not
degenerate. Equation~\eqref{eq:thetastar} is, up to normalization by
$\lVert c \rVert^2$, exactly Section~\ref{sec:coupling}'s prescription:
weight each channel's tilt by its sensitivity to the plan's economics.
Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} is the formal statement that this is the
unique minimax-optimal choice against the class of alternatives that
actually move the routing cost, and, by the Wald-type argument
underlying Theorem~\ref{thm:regret}, maximizing worst-case growth over
$\mathcal P_\Delta$ is equivalent to minimizing detection delay against
the least detectable cost-$\Delta$ drift.

\subsection{The decision layer: pluggable replanners}\label{sec:decision}

TEMPO answers \emph{when} to replan; none of Sections
\ref{sec:eprocess}--\ref{sec:theory} constrains \emph{what} the new
plan should be, and Theorems~\ref{thm:regret} and~\ref{thm:waiting} are
stated for a generic replanner $\Rmax$. This separation is deliberate:
a fleet that already runs a preferred re-optimization routine should be
able to keep it and simply gate it on TEMPO's alarm rather than on a
fixed clock. We implement three backends spanning a realistic range of
sophistication, all sharing the signature $\Rmax(\mathrm{cur},
\mathrm{remaining}, D) \to$ a new visit order, or, at the fleet level,
$\Rmax(\mathrm{curs}, \mathrm{remainings}, \mathrm{slacks}, D) \to$ a
new order per vehicle.

\emph{Single-vehicle re-sequencing.} An open-TSP construction from the
vehicle's current position by nearest-neighbor insertion followed by
2-opt local search --- the rolling-horizon workhorse, always available
and $O(m^2)$ per call.

\emph{Exact re-sequencing.} The same open-TSP problem solved to
optimality by a Miller--Tucker--Zemlin mixed-integer formulation in
Gurobi, falling back to the heuristic above beyond a size cutoff or
without a license; this is the perfect-sequencing anchor against which
the heuristic is compared in Section~\ref{sec:study}.

\emph{Fleet-wide rebalancing.} The stylized model of
Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting} is realized concretely as a 2-regret cheapest-
insertion heuristic that pools every vehicle's remaining customers and
reinserts them into open routes anchored at each vehicle's current
position, respecting each vehicle's realized residual slack, with every
resulting route finished by 2-opt. This is the only backend among the
three that can \emph{reassign} a customer across vehicles rather than
merely resequencing within one, and Section~\ref{sec:study} shows this
dimension, not sequencing quality, is where nearly all of the
re-optimization value lies.

The planning-time optimizer itself --- an adaptive large neighborhood
search --- remains available as a fourth, warm-started backend for the
full milestone-two comparison of Section~\ref{sec:study}; its
neighborhood operators assume depot-rooted routes, so the lighter
backends above serve the mid-route, partially-executed setting that a
live re-optimization call actually faces.

\section{Computational study}\label{sec:study}

\subsection{Instances, plans, and scenarios}

We evaluate on 73 instances: 40
instances of \citet{dethloff2001vehicle}, 14 instances of
\citet{salhi1999cluster}, and 19 new instances built from the real road
networks and real retail locations of five cities. For each instance we
take the longest route of the cached
adaptive-large-neighborhood-search plan under the deterministic
capacity gate, fit the null-model parameters of
Section~\ref{sec:model} from the same historical demand scenarios used
to build the plan, and rescale the route's total planned duration to
six hours, a representative single-shift last-mile route.

The grid crosses every instance with 19 scenarios (catalogued in full
in Table~\ref{tab:scenarios} in the Appendix): two null variants for
false-alarm calibration (including a hostile forecast-rain null, in
which rain is correctly forecast and hence belongs to the conditional
null of Equation~\eqref{eq:travel} rather than to any alternative),
each of the five factors at a mild and a severe step-onset magnitude,
four shape variants isolating onset profile (a slow ramp, a transient
jam that clears, and two late-onset variants), and three compound days
combining several factors at once, with magnitudes following a common
convention (traffic: log-mean multipliers; demand/dwell:
standard-deviation shifts; accident/breakdown: rate ratios). Every
instance-scenario pair is simulated for 15 independent days.

\subsection{Baselines}\label{sec:baselines}

We compare TEMPO against ten baselines spanning classical sequential
detection, the anytime-valid e-detector framework, and the
re-optimization literature's own rolling-horizon defaults.
\emph{Never-replan} rides the static plan to the end of the day (a
lower anchor). \emph{Periodic} re-solves every 40 events regardless of
evidence, matching the fixed-epoch cadence of the chance-constrained
strategy of \citet{he2026smpc}. \emph{Rolling-opt} re-solves every five
events (one stop cycle), the heuristic of \citet{gmira2021rollingopt}
against which \citet{chen2025dgta} benchmark their own reinforcement-
learning policy for the dynamic traveling salesman problem with
time-dependent stochastic travel times; retraining that policy's
Dynamic Graph Temporal Attention architecture end-to-end is a distinct,
realized-tour-cost comparison reported separately in
Section~\ref{sec:dgta}, since it has no detection layer to compare on
the metrics used here. \emph{Bonferroni-fixed} runs $z$-tests on each
channel's running mean at four pre-committed checkpoints with a
Bonferroni correction --- valid only at those exact checkpoints,
illustrating what anytime validity buys over periodic peeking.
\emph{CUSUM} \citep{page1954} and \emph{Page--Hinkley}
\citep{gama2014survey} are the classical sequential detectors, each run
per channel with a Bonferroni combination across channels; because
their false-alarm rate is not itself anytime-valid, we report both a
published-default threshold (\texttt{cusum\_cal}) and, for a fair
power comparison, a threshold oracle-calibrated on 100 null days to
match TEMPO's own realized false-alarm rate (\texttt{cusum\_match},
\texttt{ph\_match}) --- generous to the baseline, since no real
dispatcher could calibrate this way without already knowing TEMPO's
answer. \emph{e-SR and e-CUSUM} \citep{shin2024edetectors} are
e-detectors built from exactly TEMPO's own per-channel bets with the
cost-relevance tilt switched off ($s_k \equiv 1$) and no adaptive
tilt, combined by restart-and-sum (Shiryaev--Roberts) and
restart-and-max rules respectively, isolating the general
nonparametric restart machinery from TEMPO's specific coupling. As
discussed in Section~\ref{sec:related}, an e-detector's native
guarantee is an average-run-length bound over an indefinitely run
stream (their Theorem 2.4), not a finite-window false-alarm
probability bound; we verify the native guarantee separately on a
continuous, non-reset stream, and for the per-day comparison here the
two e-detectors receive the same oracle calibration as CUSUM and
Page--Hinkley, so every non-anytime-valid or improperly-calibrated
baseline gets the most favorable threshold available before TEMPO is
compared against it.

\emph{Tuning protocol.} TEMPO itself is not tuned per instance or
scenario: $\alpha = 0.05$, the fixed grids $\Theta$ and the Poisson
grid (Section~\ref{sec:eprocess}), and the EWMA rate $\beta = 0.8$ and
clip $\theta_{\max} = 2.5$ (Section~\ref{sec:tempo}) are fixed once
across the entire 73-instance, 19-scenario grid. \emph{Periodic} and
\emph{rolling-opt} use conventional literature-default cadences rather
than cadences tuned to this data. Only CUSUM, Page--Hinkley, and the
two e-detectors receive any data-dependent tuning, and it is oracle
calibration to TEMPO's own realized false-alarm rate --- a procedure no
dispatcher could run without already knowing TEMPO's answer, and one
that by construction can only help these baselines' reported detection
rate relative to an untuned deployment. Every monitor compared is an
$O(1)$-per-event streaming statistic (Section~\ref{sec:complexity}
makes this precise for TEMPO), so no asymmetry in compute time
confounds the comparison, only the calibration asymmetry just
described, which favors the baselines throughout.

\textbf{Computational complexity.}\label{sec:complexity} Each event's
update (Algorithm~\ref{alg:tempo}) is $O(J)$ per channel over the
betting grid ($J \le 4$) plus an $O(1)$ EWMA update, with $O(K)$
combination and state ($K=5$ channels), independent of route length or
elapsed horizon; pooling a fleet of $F$ vehicles raises the daily total
to $O(FmJ)$ only by processing more events, not by a costlier
per-event update. This is negligible next to any replanning backend of
Section~\ref{sec:decision}, the cheapest of which is already $O(m^2)$
per call, so the monitor is never the bottleneck of the trigger-plus-
replan pipeline.

\emph{Metrics.} For each instance-scenario-monitor triple we report the
false-alarm rate on null days (target $\le \alpha$), the detection
rate on drift days (the fraction of days on which the monitor alarms
at or after the true onset), and the mean detection delay in events,
conditional on detection.

Section~\ref{sec:ablation} isolates these components incrementally.

nonparametric restart machinery of \citet{shin2024edetectors}, built
from TEMPO's own bets without adaptive tilting or cost-relevance
weighting, already beats naive CUSUM (\texttt{demand\_ramp} 0.15 $\to$

only Section~\ref{sec:coupling}'s coupling closes --- restart-based
machinery and cost-aware betting design are separable sources of
power, and this paper's contribution is squarely the second. Detection
is uniform across geography (0.82/0.78/0.71 for TEMPO on City,
Salhi--Nagy, and Dethloff instances against \texttt{cusum\_match}'s
0.54/0.58/0.52): the margin is not an artifact of any one benchmark.

\textbf{The significance level as a price: confirming Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar}.}

Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar} predicts a U-shaped realized-cost curve in
$\alpha$ with an interior minimum. We confirm this on ten instances,
15 days each, an equal mixture of null and demand-surge days, sweeping
$\alpha$ over eight values (Table~\ref{tab:alphasweep}): realized cost
falls from $\alpha = 0.5$ to an interior minimum near $\alpha \approx
10^{-3}$ before rising again as $\alpha \to 10^{-4}$, exactly as
predicted. The two arms trace the two terms of
Equation~\eqref{eq:regret}. The right arm, at the smallest $\alpha$, is
the detection-delay term $\Delta_c(\log(1/\alpha)+\kappa)/g$: an overly
conservative monitor waits so long for evidence that the stale-plan
cost dominates. The left arm, in this harness, is not the false-alarm
cost term $\alpha C_{\mathrm{fr}}$ --- a replan on an on-model day is
here nearly free, since the insertion heuristic weakly improves the
remaining tour even absent drift, so $C_{\mathrm{fr}} \approx 0$ ---
but rather Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting}'s information term $V(s)$: at
$\alpha = 0.5$ the monitor alarms after only two or three events and
the rebalancer acts on almost no information, costing 191.1 against
156.2 on surge days at the interior optimum. A deployment in which
unnecessary replans carry a real coordination cost would show both
terms at once; we flag this as the natural next refinement of the
harness rather than a limitation of the theory.

\textbf{TEMPO complements a capacity-triggered recourse policy, and drift has a where.}

TEMPO's execution-time monitor is complementary to, and can run
alongside, any planning-layer recourse policy that intervenes only
when a vehicle's running load approaches a capacity boundary: the two
layers cover different drift regimes, and why is discussed in the
Appendix.

Figure~\ref{fig:fleet} illustrates TEMPO running on an entire fleet
rather than one vehicle: one master e-process consumes every vehicle's
events in shared clock order, so the dispatcher tests the \emph{plan},
not any single route. As the jam pocket of Section~\ref{sec:model}
opens and spreads across the real road network, evidence pooled from
vehicles whose remaining routes enter the affected zone accumulates
while vehicles routed elsewhere continue to contribute on-model
evidence, and the alarm --- fired once the pooled log e-process crosses
its threshold --- triggers a fleet-wide replan well before the day
ends; this spatial extension gives drift a location as well as a time,
which is what keeps the pooled test from being swamped by vehicles the
jam never touches. The Appendix shows the same congestion pocket
against a single planned route in isolation (Figure~\ref{fig:map}).

\textbf{Real data: the Amazon Last-Mile Routing Research Challenge.}

To check that these findings are not an artifact of synthetic demand
and travel-time models, we build a fifth evaluation family entirely
from the 2021 Amazon Last-Mile Routing Research Challenge data set
\citep{merchan2024amazon}: 50 real delivery routes, ten from each of
five metropolitan areas, 100--200 real stops each, with the null model
of Section~\ref{sec:model} fitted directly from the data set's own
planner-forecast travel times, planned service times, and package
volumes rather than from any synthetic generator.

Each route contributes 12 simulated days per scenario (50 routes
$\times$ 12 days $=$ 600 Bernoulli trials per reported rate), so we
report 95\% Wilson score confidence intervals alongside the point
estimates, computed directly from the underlying per-route outcomes.
Validity holds on real routes exactly as on synthetic ones: TEMPO's
false-alarm rate is $0.018\ [0.010, 0.033]$ on the plain null and
$0.022\ [0.013, 0.037]$ on the forecast-rain null, both within the
nominal $\alpha = 0.05$ with the upper confidence bound comfortably
below it, against $0.032\ [0.020, 0.049]$ for oracle-matched CUSUM and
$0.037\ [0.024, 0.055]$ for Page--Hinkley (whose interval's upper end
exceeds $\alpha$, consistent with its calibration guarantee being
generous but not exact). The discriminating scenario is mild,
sustained demand drift --- packages running slightly heavy all day,
without any dramatic single cause --- where TEMPO detects at $0.70\
[0.66, 0.73]$ against oracle-matched CUSUM's and Page--Hinkley's
$0.23\ [0.20, 0.27]$, non-overlapping intervals and a threefold margin;
the late-onset and ramped demand variants show the same pattern
($0.88\ [0.86, 0.91]$ against $0.82\ [0.79, 0.85]$ and $0.23\ [0.20,
0.26]$; $0.95\ [0.93, 0.96]$ against $0.93\ [0.91, 0.95]$ and $0.30\
[0.26, 0.33]$). Traffic scenarios sit at a shared ceiling for TEMPO and
CUSUM alike (0.86--0.97), since 150-stop routes carry overwhelming
travel-time evidence regardless of how it is combined, while
Page--Hinkley collapses on mild traffic drift (0.25); accident
scenarios remain information-limited ties, and compound days saturate
near 0.95 for every monitor. Detection is uniform across the five
metropolitan areas (0.84--0.86 mean). Two limitations bear noting,
neither resolved by the intervals above: the data set is
delivery-only, so a single real route geometry and one real set of
planner forecasts stand in for day-to-day dispersion across its 12
simulated days, overstating independent real-world realizations by
roughly a factor of 12; and because the data set records no
realized-versus-forecast traces, drift is still injected rather than
observed. Both are flagged for a future extension pairing multiple
realized days per route.

\textbf{Retraining DGTA-RL as a fully independent baseline.}\label{sec:dgta}

The rolling-opt baseline of Section~\ref{sec:baselines} reproduces
\citet{chen2025dgta}'s trigger cadence, not the learned policy itself
--- so we reimplement and train their DGTA network directly (the
Appendix gives the protocol) rather than cite it unrun. On matched
held-out instances, isolating the travel-time channel alone, its
realized tour cost is 36--46\% \emph{higher} than both a static
never-replan tour and TEMPO-triggered resequencing, on null and
drifted days alike --- consistent with neural constructive routing
policies' known sensitivity to training scale (the Appendix discusses
the budget), and reported as a lower bound on its achievable
performance, not a ceiling claim.

\section{Discussion and conclusion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Managerial insights}

Three implications follow directly from the theory and the
computational study, and each is stated in a form a fleet operator, not
only a statistician, can act on.

First, \emph{a dispatcher who peeks continuously does not pay a
statistical price for doing so}. This is the practical content of
Ville's inequality (Lemma~\ref{lem:ville}) and the reason
Table~\ref{tab:validity} shows classical detectors, whose guarantees
were never designed for continuous inspection, exceeding their nominal
false-alarm level in exactly the deployment pattern --- watch after
every event --- that operations naturally invite. A fleet already
monitoring its routes continuously is, whether or not it realizes it,
already violating the assumptions its CUSUM or Page--Hinkley
thresholds were tuned under; replacing the statistic, not the
monitoring cadence, closes the gap.

Second, \emph{the significance level is a dial with a defensible
setting, not a convention}. Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar}'s formula
$\alpha^\ast = \Delta_c/(g\,C_{\mathrm{fr}})$ asks only for three
numbers a fleet operations team already has some estimate of: the
per-event cost of staying on a stale plan, the monitor's empirical
evidence rate, and the coordination cost of an unnecessary
re-optimization. Cheap, software-only replans push $\alpha^\ast$ toward
one; replans requiring driver coordination push it far below the
conventional 0.05, exactly the trade-off Table~\ref{tab:alphasweep}
traces out empirically.

Third, and least intuitively, \emph{acting on a statistical alarm can
beat acting sooner}: a state-dependent replanner --- one that
reassigns customers based on realized loads rather than merely
resequencing a fixed set --- is itself better informed the later it is
invoked, up to the point where staleness costs overtake the remaining
information gain (Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting}). This departs from the
rolling-horizon literature's usual presumption that more frequent
re-optimization is weakly better, a presumption that treats replanning
as costless and information as free; neither holds once rebalancing
depends on realized, not forecast, residual capacity.

TEMPO adds no new fitted model beyond what an optimal-stopping
planning layer already produces for its own recourse decisions ---
the continuation cost $\widehat C(\cdot)$ is read, not re-estimated ---
so a fleet that has already adopted such a policy acquires an
anytime-valid re-optimization trigger at negligible additional cost.

\textbf{Limitations, collected.} The sensitivity proxies of
Section~\ref{sec:coupling} and Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}'s alternative
class are approximations chosen for tractability rather than derived
necessities; the channel-validity arguments require the null model's
channels to be correctly specified conditional on observed covariates
(Remark~\ref{rem:independence}); and Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting} is
proved in a deliberately stylized model rather than for the
fleet-scale rebalancer actually used. Each is discussed at the point it
arises, and in the Appendix; none affects Section~\ref{sec:tempo}'s
validity guarantees, which hold regardless of proxy quality --- they
bound only how much of the demonstrated \emph{power} should be
attributed to these specific closed forms versus to the general
coupling principle they instantiate.

\textbf{Conclusion.}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have posed the timing of vehicle-routing re-optimization as a
sequential hypothesis test and shown that anytime-valid testing,
coupled to the routing plan's own economics through predictable
sensitivity tilts, answers it with both a statistical guarantee and an
economic interpretation that neither periodic re-optimization nor
classical sequential detection offers. The coupling is free of
statistical cost by construction
(Propositions~\ref{prop:coupledvalidity}, \ref{prop:dualregime},
and~\ref{prop:split}), and is provably the right design against the
class of alternatives that move routing cost
(Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}), not merely a plausible one. The resulting
monitor, TEMPO, together with a regret bound
(Theorem~\ref{thm:regret}), a closed-form significance level
(Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar}), and a theorem explaining why waiting
for evidence can beat replanning immediately
(Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting}), gives a dispatcher a defensible answer to
a question current practice answers by convention or by clock. Across
73 synthetic instances, a 19-scenario drift grid, and a real-data
pilot, TEMPO detects harmful drift faster and more reliably than every
one of ten baselines at a matched false-alarm budget, and triggering a
fleet-wide rebalance on its alarm realizes a further six percentage
points of cost saving over replanning at the first sign of trouble.

Three extensions follow from the limitations noted along the way. The
channel-wise independence factorizing Equation~\eqref{eq:master} is a
modeling convenience broken by any common driver of two channels at
once; a spatially correlated congestion field beyond the rain case
handled here would require betting against a genuinely joint
conditional law, which the chain-rule construction of
Section~\ref{sec:eprocess} accommodates at the cost of a richer null
model. The stylized two-vehicle model behind Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting}
could be extended to an arbitrary fleet size and general breach-cost
function, at the cost of a less closed-form $V(s)$. Finally, crossing
every trigger with a full warm-started adaptive-large-neighborhood-
search replan across the entire 19-scenario grid, rather than the
lightweight replanners used in Section~\ref{sec:study}, is the natural
next computational study, already supported by the pluggable interface
of Section~\ref{sec:decision}.

\begin{APPENDIX}{Proofs of Selected Results}\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:dualregime} (validity of the dual-regime statistic)}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
We first show $\prod_k E_k(n)$ is itself an e-process, and deliberately
\emph{without} invoking any cross-channel independence assumption,
because none is needed: by construction (Section~\ref{sec:model}),
exactly one channel is realized at every event $n$. Consequently, at
any event $n$ with $\mathrm{channel}(n) = k^\ast$, every factor
$E_j(n-1)$ for $j \ne k^\ast$ is already $\F_{n-1}$-measurable (it did
not update at $n$, by definition of $E_j$), and only $E_{k^\ast}(n) =
E_{k^\ast}(n-1)\, e_{k^\ast}(n)$ contains genuine randomness at $n$.
Hence
\begin{align*}
&\E_{P_0}\Bigl[\textstyle\prod\nolimits_k E_k(n) \,\Big|\, \F_{n-1}\Bigr] \\
&\quad= \Bigl(\textstyle\prod\nolimits_{j \ne k^\ast} E_j(n-1)\Bigr)\,
E_{k^\ast}(n-1) \\
&\qquad {}\times \E_{P_0}\bigl[e_{k^\ast}(n) \mid \F_{n-1}\bigr] \\
&\quad= \textstyle\prod\nolimits_k E_k(n-1),
\end{align*}
using only that the $F_{n-1}$-measurable factors pull out of the
conditional expectation and that $\E_{P_0}[e_{k^\ast}(n) \mid \F_{n-1}]
= 1$ by Proposition~\ref{prop:coupledvalidity} applied to the single
channel that actually fires. This is the same asynchronous-update
argument already used in the proof of
Proposition~\ref{prop:validity}, applied here to a product of
per-channel running processes rather than to a single running process;
no assumption about the joint law across channels --- independent or
otherwise --- enters at any step, because at most one channel's
conditional distribution is ever queried at a given $n$. The simple
average $\tfrac{1}{4}\sum_k E_k(n)$ is an e-process by
Lemma~\ref{lem:mixture} directly (each $E_k(n)$ already is one). The
convex combination of the two, Equation~\eqref{eq:dualregime}, is
therefore an e-value by Lemma~\ref{lem:mixture} applied one further
time, hence $(C_n)$ is a nonnegative supermartingale with
$\E_{P_0}[C_n] \le 1$, and the tail bound is again Lemma~\ref{lem:ville}.
\Halmos
\end{proof}

\subsection{On the independence assumption (Remark~\ref{rem:independence})}

Proposition~\ref{prop:dualregime}'s validity is unconditional on
whether the five factors are correlated with one another in reality;
it holds as long as each channel's null law, \emph{as specified in
Section~\ref{sec:model}}, is the correct conditional law of that
channel given the full history $\F_{n-1}$ at the moment it fires. This
is a substantive assumption about model correctness, not a mathematical
loophole: if two channels are driven by an unmodeled common factor ---
for instance, if heavy rain elevates both travel times and accident
rates, but only the travel-time null (Equation~\eqref{eq:travel})
conditions on the rain indicator $R$ while the accident null
(Equation~\eqref{eq:accident}) does not --- then observing unusually
high realized travel times is, in reality, informative about the
accident channel's true conditional distribution in a way our
specification does not capture, and the guarantee is only as good as
the null model's fidelity to that dependence. Our construction avoids
this specific failure mode for the one common exogenous factor the
world model includes (rain modulates only the travel-time null, and
does so exactly, so no other channel's conditional law is
misspecified by omitting it), but a fuller model with, say, a
spatio-temporally correlated congestion field that also drives
accident rates would need every affected channel's null to condition
on that field explicitly, exactly as the travel-time channel
conditions on $R$ here. We flag this as a modeling-fidelity question
distinct from, and not resolved by, the martingale argument above, and
return to it in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:segments} (segment-wise validity under reset)}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
Fix $i$ and condition on $\F_{\tau_{i-1}}$, the information available
at the start of segment $i$ (whatever it is --- in particular, however
it depends on segments $1,\dots,i-1$). By construction, segment $i$'s
combined statistic $(C_n)_{n > \tau_{i-1}}$ is a fresh e-process
restarted at value $1$ against the conditional null specified by the
replanned state at $\tau_{i-1}$: that is, $\E_{P_0}[C_{\tau_{i-1}+1}
\mid \F_{\tau_{i-1}}] \le 1$ and each subsequent step satisfies the
same conditional-mean-one property by
Propositions~\ref{prop:coupledvalidity} and~\ref{prop:dualregime}
applied with $\F_{\tau_{i-1}}$ in place of $\F_0$. Ville's inequality
(Lemma~\ref{lem:ville}) is itself proved by an application of Markov's
inequality to a nonnegative supermartingale via the tower property, and
that proof goes through conditionally, for any starting
$\sigma$-algebra, verbatim: $\Prob_{P_0}\bigl(\exists\, n > \tau_{i-1} :
C_n \ge 2/\alpha \text{ or } E_{\mathrm{brk}} \ge 2/\alpha \,\big|\,
\F_{\tau_{i-1}}\bigr) \le \alpha$ almost surely, for whatever
$\F_{\tau_{i-1}}$ turns out to be. Taking expectations of this a.s.\
bound over $\F_{\tau_{i-1}}$ gives the unconditional bound
$\Prob_{P_0}(A_i) \le \alpha$. This did not use independence between
$A_i$ and the earlier segments at any point --- only that the
restarted process is, conditionally, an e-process from $\F_{\tau_{i-1}}$
onward, which is true by the construction of the reset regardless of
how $\F_{\tau_{i-1}}$ was reached. Summing
$\Prob_{P_0}(A_i) \le \alpha$ over $i = 1, \dots, R$ and applying
linearity of expectation to the count $\sum_{i=1}^R \mathbf{1}\{A_i\}$
gives the stated bound on the expected number of alarms. \Halmos
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:regret} (cost-regret of the triggered replan)}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
Partition on whether TEMPO alarms before or after the true change-point.

\emph{Case $\tau \ge \nu$ (no false alarm before $\nu$).} On this event
both TEMPO and the oracle are on-model, and hence identical, until
$\nu$; after $\nu$, TEMPO continues to execute the stale plan for
$\tau - \nu$ further events before invoking $\Rmax$, while the oracle
invokes $\Rmax$ immediately at $\nu$. By Assumption~\ref{ass:staleness},
each of these $\tau - \nu$ stale events costs at most $\Delta_c$ more
than it would under the (already replanned) oracle path, and once both
policies have invoked the identical $\Rmax$ their subsequent costs
coincide; telescoping over the $\tau - \nu$ stale events bounds the
cost difference on this event by $\Delta_c (\tau - \nu)$.

It remains to bound $\E[(\tau - \nu)^+ \mid \tau \ge \nu]$, and here we
give a complete argument rather than an appeal to informal analogy,
distinguishing the two betting regimes of Section~\ref{sec:tempo}.

For the fixed-grid mixture bet, and under the iid-post-change reading
of Assumption~\ref{ass:kl}, the log-increments $Y_j := \log
e^{\mathrm{g}}(\theta_j^\ast; z_{\nu+j})$ of the optimal grid component
after $\nu$ are i.i.d.\ under $P_1$ with mean $g > 0$ by
Assumption~\ref{ass:growth} and finite variance, since each $Y_j$ is
almost surely bounded by $\max_j |\theta_j z - \tfrac12 \theta_j^2|$
over the finite grid $\Theta$ (a bounded random variable has finite
moments of every order). The quantity $\tau - \nu$ is exactly the
first-passage time of the random walk $S_j = \sum_{i=1}^j Y_i$ over the
level $\log(1/\alpha)$, which is almost surely finite by the strong law
of large numbers ($g>0$ implies $S_j \to \infty$). Wald's identity
\citep{wald1945sequential} applied to this a.s.-finite stopping time
gives $\E[S_{\tau-\nu}] = g\,\E[\tau-\nu]$; since $S_{\tau-\nu} =
\log(1/\alpha) + \big(S_{\tau-\nu} - \log(1/\alpha)\big)$ and the second
term is the (nonnegative) overshoot of the random walk past its
boundary, Lorden's classical inequality for the overshoot of a random
walk with positive drift \citep{lorden1970excess} bounds its
expectation by $\kappa := \E[Y_1^2]/(2g) < \infty$, the finiteness
following from the boundedness of $Y_1$ established above. Combining,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wald}
\E[(\tau - \nu)^+ \mid \tau \ge \nu] \ \le\ \frac{\log(1/\alpha) +
\kappa}{g}.
\end{equation}

The adaptive bet of Equation~\eqref{eq:adaptivebet} complicates this
argument only technically, not in substance: because its tilt
$\widehat\theta_k(n)$ depends on the EWMA of past residuals, its
log-increments are no longer i.i.d., so Wald's identity does not apply
verbatim. The sequence $(\log E_{\nu+j} - jg)_{j \ge 0}$ is nonetheless
a submartingale with increments bounded almost surely by the clipped
tilt range $[0,\theta_{\max}]$ (Equation~\eqref{eq:ewma}), and the
identical overshoot argument generalizes to this setting via nonlinear
renewal theory \citep{woodroofe1982nonlinear}, which extends Lorden's
bound to stopped sums of dependent, uniformly bounded increments with a
uniform lower bound on conditional drift; the resulting bound has the
same form as Equation~\eqref{eq:wald}, with $\kappa$ now governed by
the uniform per-step increment bound $\theta_{\max}$ rather than a
fixed second moment. In either regime $\kappa$ is a finite,
well-defined constant determined entirely by the betting grid, and
Equation~\eqref{eq:wald} holds. Combining, the expected cost difference
on $\{\tau \ge \nu\}$ is at most $\Delta_c (\log(1/\alpha)+\kappa)/g$.

\emph{Case $\tau < \nu$ (a false alarm before the true change).} By
Proposition~\ref{prop:validity} (or~\ref{prop:split} for the assembled
monitor), $\Prob_{P_0}(\tau < \nu) \le \Prob_{P_0}(\tau < \infty) \le
\alpha$. On this event, the day is on-model until $\nu$, so the only
difference between TEMPO and the oracle up to $\nu$ is one unnecessary
invocation of $\Rmax$, costing at most $C_{\mathrm{fr}}$; after $\nu$,
TEMPO's reset (Section~\ref{sec:tempo}) restarts a fresh e-process
against the new plan's conditional null, and the same delay bound of
Equation~\eqref{eq:wald} applies to this new segment, contributing a
term of the same order as the first case and hence not affecting the
bound's order. The expected contribution of this branch to the overall
regret is therefore at most $\Prob_{P_0}(\tau < \nu) \cdot
C_{\mathrm{fr}} \le \alpha\, C_{\mathrm{fr}}$, absorbing the
second-segment delay term into the same constant.

Summing the two cases' contributions to the expected regret gives
Equation~\eqref{eq:regret}. \Halmos
\end{proof}

\subsection{The delta-method derivation of Equation~\eqref{eq:vs} (value of waiting)}

The stylized model of Section~\ref{sec:theory} (two vehicles, split
fraction $\rho^\ast(\mu)$, Equation~\eqref{eq:optsplit}) sets up the
following derivation.

\eqref{eq:optsplit} by the same order. We complete the
delta-method argument explicitly rather than leave it asserted. Let
$\Phi(\rho)$ denote the expected combined breach cost of assigning
fraction $\rho$ of the remaining pool to $A$, twice continuously
differentiable near $\rho^\ast(\mu)$ with $\Phi'(\rho^\ast(\mu)) = 0$
(the population optimum is interior and stationary) and
$\Phi''(\rho^\ast(\mu)) > 0$ (a strict local minimum). A second-order
Taylor expansion of $\Phi$ around the true optimum gives
\begin{align}
\Phi(\widehat\rho(s)) - \Phi(\rho^\ast(\mu))
&\approx \tfrac12\, \Phi''(\rho^\ast(\mu)) \notag \\
&\quad {}\times \bigl(\widehat\rho(s) - \rho^\ast(\mu)\bigr)^2,
\label{eq:phiexpand}
\end{align}
since the first-order term vanishes at a stationary point. A first-order
Taylor expansion of $\rho^\ast(\cdot)$ itself, which is smooth in $\mu$
by its closed form~\eqref{eq:optsplit} with derivative $(\rho^\ast)'(\mu)
= -(Y_A - Y_B)/\bigl(2(n-s)\mu^2\bigr)$, gives $\widehat\rho(s) -
\rho^\ast(\mu) \approx (\rho^\ast)'(\mu)\,\bigl(\widehat\mu(s) -
\mu\bigr)$. Since $\widehat\mu(s)$ is the mean of $s$ i.i.d.\
post-change observations, $\E\bigl[(\widehat\mu(s)-\mu)^2\bigr] =
\sigma^2/s$ exactly (the sampling variance of a sample mean), so taking
expectations of Equation~\eqref{eq:phiexpand} gives
\begin{align}
V(s) &:= \E\bigl[\Phi(\widehat\rho(s))\bigr] - \Phi(\rho^\ast(\mu))
\ \approx\ \frac{\kappa_0\, \sigma^2}{s}, \notag\\
\kappa_0 &:= \tfrac12\, \Phi''(\rho^\ast(\mu))\,
\bigl[(\rho^\ast)'(\mu)\bigr]^2, \notag
\end{align}
an explicit, computable constant given the breach-cost curvature
$\Phi''$ and the closed-form split sensitivity $(\rho^\ast)'(\mu)$;
both are, in principle, available from the same fleet-slack data
$(Y_A, Y_B, n-s)$ that defines $\rho^\ast$ itself, though we do not fit
$\Phi$ explicitly in the computational study and instead treat
$\kappa_0$ as a free positive constant, exactly as $\Delta_c$ and $g$
are treated in Theorem~\ref{thm:regret}. $V(s)$ is strictly decreasing
in $s$, formalizing the intuition that information about $\mu$
accrues, and the value of a plug-in estimate improves, the longer the
rebalancer waits. We emphasize what this derivation does and does not
establish: it is a local (small-estimation-error), single-parameter
(scalar $\mu$) approximation for a two-vehicle, single-rebalancing-
decision model: a real fleet rebalancer solves a combinatorial
reinsertion problem over many customers and many routes
(Section~\ref{sec:decision}), and Equation~\eqref{eq:vs} is offered as
the simplest model in which the qualitative mechanism --- more
realized information lowers the expected cost of a state-dependent
decision --- can be made exact, not as a claim that Equation
\eqref{eq:vs} literally governs the reinsertion heuristic used in
Section~\ref{sec:study}.

\subsection{On the alternative class $\mathcal P_\Delta$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:growth})}

$\mathcal P_\Delta$ is a modeling choice, not a derived object, and we
state plainly why we make it rather than leave it to be inferred: it is
the routing-cost analogue of the contamination and shrinking
neighborhoods long used to define worst-case performance in robust
statistics, where an estimator or test is designed to be optimal
against the \emph{least favorable} member of a constraint set rather
than against every conceivable alternative
\citep{huber1964robust}. Just as Huber's minimax framework does not
claim that every real deviation from a parametric model lies exactly on
its contamination neighborhood's boundary, we do not claim every real
drift lies exactly on the hyperplane $\mathcal P_\Delta$; the theorem
below characterizes the tilt that is optimal \emph{against the worst
case within this class}, and the class is chosen to formalize
``alternatives with a specified linearized cost impact'' in the
simplest possible way, a linear constraint on the same cost-gradient
vector $c$ that Section~\ref{sec:coupling} already uses to build the
sensitivity proxies. A different, non-linear specification of ``equal
cost impact'' would define a different, generally curved constraint
set and could in principle favor a different tilt direction; we see no
evidence in the computational study
(Section~\ref{sec:study}) that this matters in practice --- the
detection-rate margins of Table~\ref{tab:detection} are consistent with
the linearized theory throughout the drift magnitudes tested --- but we
do not claim the linear class is forced upon us by the routing problem
itself, only that it is the natural first-order one, exactly as
Huber's neighborhoods are the natural first candidates for robust
location estimation rather than the only conceivable ones.

\subsection{On the demand-sensitivity proxy (Equation~\eqref{eq:sensdemand})}

Equation~\eqref{eq:truesens} requires the fitted derivative $\widehat
C'(W_{n-1})$ at every event, which is an additional quantity beyond
what the planning layer already reports. We instead use the
closed-form proxy of Equation~\eqref{eq:sensdemand},
\begin{equation}\notag
s_{\mathrm{demand}}(n) = 1 - \min\!\left(\frac{|B - W_{n-1}|}{B}, 1\right),
\end{equation}
which coincides with $r(n)$'s qualitative behavior --- decreasing in
distance from a boundary, saturating away from it --- under two
simplifying conditions we state explicitly rather than leave implicit:
(i) the stopping boundary $\{D = 0\}$ lies near the physical capacity
$B$, so that $B$ itself is a usable proxy for the boundary's location
without fitting $D$ directly; and (ii) the product $\widehat
C'(W)\,\sigma_{g,n}$ that rescales true distance into shock units in
Equation~\eqref{eq:truesens} is approximately constant along the route,
so that raw distance and rescaled distance are monotone transforms of
one another. Both conditions hold by construction in the
optimal-stopping recourse model of Section~\ref{sec:model} (the
boundary is a level set of $W$ at $B$, and $\sigma_{g,n}$ is drawn from
a common historical distribution across stops), but need not hold in
every routing model, and we flag this as a stated limitation rather
than a hidden one: a planning layer whose fitted $\widehat C(\cdot)$ is
strongly nonlinear, or whose demand volatility varies sharply across
stops, would be better served by computing Equation~\eqref{eq:truesens}
directly from the fitted value function's own derivative, at the
implementation cost of exposing that derivative to the monitor. Both
$D(W_{n-1})$ and $\widehat C'(W_{n-1})$, when used, are functions of
information realized strictly before event $n$ and hence
$\F_{n-1}$-measurable, so Proposition~\ref{prop:coupledvalidity} below
covers either choice identically; the two differ only in how closely
they track the growth-optimal direction of Theorem~\ref{thm:growth},
never in validity.

\subsection{On the travel/dwell/accident sensitivity's uniform-penalty limitation (Equation~\eqref{eq:sensrest})}

We flag
the same class of limitation as for demand: Equation~\eqref{eq:sensrest}
weights every downstream stop equally, so it correctly captures how
\emph{many} stops a shock threatens but not how much any one of them
individually costs to miss. A stop with a hard time window and a high
penalty contributes the same weight as an ordinary stop with slack;
the reviewer-facing failure mode this produces --- a single, tightly
windowed, high-value stop near the route's end receiving too little
weight because the remaining-fraction $(m-k+1)/m$ is small there even
though its own marginal cost is large --- is real, and the correct
refinement is a per-stop-penalty-weighted downstream sum rather than a
raw count, computable if per-stop time-window and penalty data are
attached to each route (our harness, following
Section~\ref{sec:model}, does not model time windows, so we cannot
fit or evaluate that refinement here without introducing an entirely
new set of null-model parameters, and we say so rather than
implicitly assume a homogeneous-penalty world is the general case).

\subsection{On Assumption~\ref{ass:growth} (Remark~\ref{rem:growth})}

$g > 0$ is not a technical convenience but the minimal identifiability
condition any test of $P_0$ against $P_1$ must satisfy: if every grid
tilt had zero or negative expected log-growth under $P_1$, no
betting-based test --- ours or any competitor's --- could detect the
change faster than chance, since $g \le 0$ is equivalent to the
best available bet being unable to distinguish $P_1$ from $P_0$ in the
direction the grid searches. For the fixed grid this is a property of
the grid and the true alternative, verifiable in simulation
(Section~\ref{sec:study} reports $g$ implicitly through the realized
detection delays) but not guaranteed by construction for an arbitrary,
unanticipated $P_1$: a change whose direction is orthogonal to every
grid tilt is, correctly, not detected by this term of the theorem
(this is exactly the information-limited regime documented for the
mild accident scenario in Table~\ref{tab:detection}). For the adaptive
bet, $g \to \KL(P_1 \Vert P_0)$ is a stated \emph{consistency} property
of the aGRAPA estimator under the regularity conditions of
\citet{waudbysmithramdas2024} (bounded increments and a persistently
exciting residual stream), not an additional assumption we introduce;
Theorem~\ref{thm:regret}'s bound is accordingly conditional on this
convergence having approximately occurred by the time $\tau$ is
reached, which the dual-regime combination of
Section~\ref{sec:tempo} is designed to make robust to but which the
theorem does not itself certify for small post-change sample sizes.

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} (growth-optimality of the decision-relevant tilt)}

\begin{proof}{Proof}
For fixed $\theta$, the map $\eta \mapsto \sum_k \theta_k \eta_k$ is
linear, and $\mathcal P_\Delta$ is an affine hyperplane (unbounded
whenever $K \ge 2$) rather than a bounded set; if $\theta$ has any
component orthogonal to $c$ --- that is, if $\theta \ne \lambda c$ for
any scalar $\lambda$ --- one may move $\eta$ within $\mathcal P_\Delta$
along the direction orthogonal to $c$ on which $\theta$ has a nonzero
component, holding $c^\top\eta = \Delta$ fixed, while driving $\sum_k
\theta_k \eta_k \to -\infty$; since $\psi_k(\theta_k)$ does not depend
on $\eta$, this drives $\gamma(\theta) \to -\infty$. Hence $\gamma(\theta)$
is finite only if $\theta = \lambda c$ for some $\lambda \in \mathbb
R$. For such $\theta$, $\theta^\top \eta = \lambda\, c^\top \eta =
\lambda \Delta$ for \emph{every} $\eta \in \mathcal P_\Delta$ --- the
worst case over the entire hyperplane collapses to a single value,
because the only component of $\eta$ that matters to the growth is its
projection onto $c$, which is fixed at $\Delta/\lVert c\rVert$ on the
hyperplane --- so
\[
\gamma(\lambda c) = \lambda \Delta - \sum_k \psi_k(\lambda c_k).
\]
This is a concave function of the scalar $\lambda$ (each $\psi_k$ is
convex, being a log-moment-generating function), maximized where
$\Delta = \sum_k c_k\, \psi_k'(\lambda^\ast c_k) = c^\top
\nabla\psi(\lambda^\ast c)$, the first-order condition stated in the
theorem. For the Gaussian channels, $\psi_k'(\theta) = \theta$, so the
condition reads $\Delta = \lambda^\ast \lVert c \rVert^2$, giving
$\lambda^\ast = \Delta/\lVert c\rVert^2$ and $\theta_k^\ast = \lambda^\ast
c_k = \Delta c_k /\lVert c\rVert^2$, Equation~\eqref{eq:thetastar}.
\Halmos
\end{proof}

\subsection{Why the sensitivity tilt alone can hurt before adaptive betting is added (Table~\ref{tab:ablation})}

The mechanism is exactly what Section~\ref{sec:coupling}'s derivation
predicts: the demand sensitivity $s_{\mathrm{demand}}(n)$ is small
whenever the running load sits far from the capacity boundary,
correctly reflecting low decision-relevance, but a \emph{fixed} grid
tilt scaled down by a small $s(n)$ is also a \emph{weaker} statistical
bet in absolute terms, and early-to-mid-route demand drift generates
most of its evidence exactly where $s(n)$ is smallest. Only once the
tilt is allowed to adapt --- learning the drift's true size online
rather than relying on a fixed grid scaled by a proxy --- does the
combination recover and exceed the untilted baseline. The sensitivity
weighting is thus only unambiguously beneficial once paired with a bet
that can recover the strength it removes, which the dual-regime
combination then compounds further on single-channel drift, where the
mean-isolation term matters most (\texttt{demand\_severe}
\texttt{tilt\_adapt}$\to$\texttt{tempo2}: $0.538 \to 0.756$, against a
near-ceiling, marginal gain on the already-multi-channel
\texttt{rush\_crush} scenario, $0.991 \to 0.996$, as
Section~\ref{sec:tempo}'s account of the mean term predicts). On
\texttt{traffic\_late} and \texttt{traffic\_mild}, where travel-time
drift is decision-relevant everywhere along the route
(Equation~\eqref{eq:sensrest} is bounded away from zero throughout), the
tilt alone already helps and adaptive betting and the dual regime each
add a further, smaller increment --- a simpler interaction than the
demand case, again consistent with the mechanism rather than merely
favorable to it.

\subsection{DGTA-RL reimplementation, training, and matched-instance evaluation details (Section~\ref{sec:dgta})}

We reimplement the dual-attention encoder and spatial-temporal pointer
decoder of \citet{chen2025dgta} at reduced scale (hidden dimension 64,
four attention heads, two dual-attention and two dynamic-encoder
blocks) and train it by REINFORCE with a frozen, periodically-updated
baseline network per the original paper's Algorithm 1: 1{,}000 epochs,
batch size 128, single-vehicle instances of 20 customers drawn uniform
on the unit square, travel times realized under the original paper's
own time-dependent Gamma process (Equation 3 of \citealp{chen2025dgta}).
Training runs entirely on CPU in this environment (no GPU was used),
and the baseline network is updated eight times over the run on a
paired $t$-test ($p<0.05$) against a held-out evaluation set, with
mean realized tour cost falling from 24.9 to 19.5 (arbitrary time
units) over training --- a real, if modest, improvement given the
budget.

The matched-instance comparison against TEMPO evaluates three policies
on 40 held-out instances (disjoint from training) $\times$ 12
independent days: a static never-replan tour (nearest-neighbor
construction plus 2-opt, executed to completion); TEMPO-triggered
resequencing (the real \texttt{TempoMonitor} of
Section~\ref{sec:tempo}, fed only the travel-time channel, triggering
the same nearest-neighbor-plus-2-opt replanner on alarm, repriced using
the currently observable congestion extent); and the trained DGTA-RL
policy's greedy rollout. All three consume identical realized
travel-time draws under TEMPO's own null-and-drift model
(Equation~\eqref{eq:travel}) rather than DGTA-RL's native training-time
process, isolating the single travel-time channel DGTA-RL models (it
has no demand or capacity dimension) --- the same protocol every other
Section~\ref{sec:study} baseline receives: calibrated on its own native
convention, then compared to TEMPO on TEMPO's shared grid. The drift is
a transient zonal congestion pocket (Section~\ref{sec:model}'s spatial
extension, magnitude 2.5, radius growing from 0.25 to 1.15 over a
six-hour window before fully clearing), not a uniform slowdown, since
scaling every edge by one constant cannot be exploited by resequencing
at all and would make the comparison vacuous by construction.

Realized cost (mean over $40 \times 12 = 480$ days per condition):
never-replan 11.76 (null) / 14.67 (drifted); TEMPO-triggered
resequencing 11.68 / 14.75; DGTA-RL 17.22 / 20.06. TEMPO's resequencing
edge over the static tour is small and not statistically distinguishable
here (a lightweight nearest-neighbor-plus-2-opt resequencer has far
less reassignment flexibility than the fleet-wide rebalancer behind
Table~\ref{tab:replan}'s result), and we report this plainly rather
than only the flattering comparison. DGTA-RL, by contrast, is 36--46\%
\emph{more} costly than either construction-heuristic baseline on both
the null and drifted days, a wide and consistent gap given the sample
size. We attribute this primarily to the constrained training budget
(1{,}000 REINFORCE epochs on CPU, versus the substantially larger-scale
training neural constructive routing policies typically require to
match strong heuristics in this literature) rather than to a claim
about the architecture's ceiling performance; the result should be
read as a lower bound on what a fully-trained DGTA-RL could achieve,
not as evidence the published method cannot match classical heuristics
given adequate compute.

\subsection{Why TEMPO and a capacity-triggered recourse policy cover different drift regimes (Section~\ref{sec:study})}

On a demand-surge day, both layers react: a capacity-triggered
policy's continuation-cost boundary is approached and it intervenes,
while TEMPO's demand channel accumulates evidence in step. On a
traffic-jam day, only TEMPO fires --- the jam never pushes any
vehicle's load toward a capacity breach, so a purely capacity-triggered
policy correctly stays silent even as the plan's realized lateness
climbs steadily, a drift a load-based policy has no state to see. A
capacity-triggered policy prices \emph{what to do} once a capacity
constraint is in play; TEMPO decides \emph{when} the plan --- any part
of it, capacity-related or not --- has stopped matching reality.

\subsection{What is routine and what is new in Section~\ref{sec:theory} (economics of the trigger)}

Every individual probabilistic tool Section~\ref{sec:theory} invokes
--- Ville's inequality, Wald's identity, the overshoot bounds of
\citet{lorden1970excess} and \citet{woodroofe1982nonlinear}, and
elementary convex calculus --- is classical, and
Propositions~\ref{prop:validity}--\ref{prop:split} are, individually,
routine consequences of the tower property and the union bound, stated
for completeness rather than as claims of technical difficulty; a
paper that borrows well-established machinery should not be mistaken
for one that reinvents it. What is genuinely new is the
\emph{composition}: Theorem~\ref{thm:regret} is, to our knowledge, the
first bound translating a sequential test's detection-delay guarantee
directly into \emph{realized routing cost} rather than sample size or
type-I/II error; Corollary~\ref{cor:alphastar} is the closed-form
significance level that composition implies, economic in content (a
formula computable from three quantities fleet operators already
track) rather than mathematical; Theorem~\ref{thm:waiting} formalizes,
in a deliberately stylized model, why an evidence-gated trigger can
dominate an immediate one when the downstream decision is itself
statistical; and Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}, the paper's central
methodological claim, shows that Section~\ref{sec:coupling}'s
decision-relevant sensitivity weights --- introduced there as a
modeling choice --- are in fact the unique growth-optimal weights
against a natural class of cost-relevant alternatives, not merely one
heuristic choice among many.

\subsection{Two further findings on the value of waiting (Table~\ref{tab:replan})}

Exact re-sequencing matches the lightweight nearest-neighbor-plus-2-opt
heuristic to within 0.1\%: under demand drift, sequencing quality is
nearly irrelevant, and essentially all of the re-optimization value
lies in the ability to \emph{reassign} customers across vehicles,
which only the fleet-wide rebalancer of Section~\ref{sec:decision} can
do. And every backend of Section~\ref{sec:decision} is a drop-in
replacement for any other, confirming that trigger and replanner
compose as designed.
