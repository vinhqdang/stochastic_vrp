\title[Optimal Stopping for Preventive Vehicle Handoff]{Optimal stopping for
preventive vehicle handoff in stochastic routing with simultaneous pickup
and delivery}

\abstract{In last-mile operations with simultaneous pickup and delivery,
the load of a vehicle fluctuates non-monotonically along its route, and a
capacity breach can occur in the middle of a tour even when the final load
is comfortably below capacity. Operators can preempt such breaches by
handing the remainder of a route to a standby vehicle at moderate cost,
whereas an unplanned breach forces an expensive emergency dispatch and
degrades service for every remaining customer. We study the resulting
per-route execution problem as a finite-horizon optimal stopping problem
and make three contributions. First, we show that the natural but naive
supervised formulation, which labels a route by whether its \emph{final}
load exceeds capacity, is systematically biased: the endpoint event is a
strict subset of the physical mid-route breach event, and on
collect-then-deliver structures the bias is total, driving any
threshold policy trained on such labels to inaction. Second, we propose
OTR-2.0, which combines peak-aware labels with a Longstaff--Schwartz
regression scheme that estimates the cost of continuing optimally at every
stop; the resulting trigger compares this continuation estimate with the
state-dependent price of handing off now and requires no threshold tuning.
We prove that the induced stopping rule is optimal for the fitted models,
that the continuation value is monotone in the running load (justifying
isotonic regression), and that the fixed-threshold rule of prior practice
over-triggers early in a route. Third, we evaluate the policy on the 40
Dethloff benchmark instances under planning policies of varying
conservatism and under a realistic last-mile cost model with contracted
fleets, standby handoffs priced on the remaining route, and surge-priced
emergency vehicles with service-level compensation. Across synthetic
structural scenarios the method recovers 52--88\% of the reactive
execution cost where the endpoint-trained policy recovers between 0\% and
86\%, and on the benchmark it closes the largest share of the gap to a
clairvoyant lower bound among all evaluated policies.}

\keywords{stochastic vehicle routing, simultaneous pickup and delivery,
optimal stopping, Longstaff--Schwartz, isotonic regression, last-mile
logistics}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Urban last-mile operators increasingly run \emph{simultaneous pickup and
delivery} services: the same vehicle that delivers parcels also collects
returns, marketplace consignments, or reverse-logistics items along the
tour \citep{min1989multiple,dethloff2001vehicle,koc2020review}. Because
deliveries free capacity while pickups consume it, the on-board load of
such a vehicle is a non-monotone stochastic process, and the moment of
greatest stress is typically in the \emph{middle} of the route rather than
at its end. When realised pickup volumes run above plan, the vehicle may
reach a customer it can no longer serve. At that point the operator has no
good options left: an ad-hoc replacement vehicle must be hired at surge
prices, the remaining customers are served late, and service-level
compensation accrues --- the situation faced daily by ride-hailing and
delivery platforms operating gig-economy fleets.

The mitigation available \emph{before} the breach is a \emph{planned
handoff}: at some stop, the remaining workload is transferred to a standby
vehicle from a pooled reserve at a known, moderate price. The operational
question is deceptively simple: \emph{at which stop, if any, should a
vehicle give up and hand off?} Acting too early wastes standby capacity on
routes that would have completed; acting too late converts cheap planned
recourse into expensive emergencies. The decision must be made online,
stop by stop, from the realised load trajectory alone.

This paper studies that decision as a finite-horizon optimal stopping
problem and develops a data-driven policy, \emph{OTR-2.0} (Online
Threshold-free Reassignment), that is trained offline on historical load
paths and executed online with negligible per-stop computation. Our
starting point is a diagnosis of why the natural supervised approach
fails. A first-generation system deployed by our industrial partner ---
referred to as v1 throughout --- estimated, at each stop, the conditional
probability that the route would end in overflow, and handed off when that
probability crossed a tuned threshold $\tau$. Field results were
disappointing, and the root cause turned out to be twofold. The training
label declared a route an overflow when its \emph{endpoint} net load
exceeded the slack; but on routes where pickups precede deliveries the
endpoint total cancels toward zero while the mid-route peak breaches
capacity, so the labels were almost surely zero, the estimated curves
collapsed, and the policy never triggered. And even with correct labels, a
single constant threshold ignores \emph{option value}: early in a route,
waiting is nearly free because many future opportunities to hand off
remain, whereas late in a route it is not --- no constant $\tau$ has the
right shape, and tuning one on a grid overfits the training history.

OTR-2.0 repairs both defects. Labels are computed from the \emph{running
peak} of the load path, which is the physical breach event. The threshold
is abolished altogether: following the least-squares Monte-Carlo tradition
of American-option pricing
\citep{longstaff2001valuing,tsitsiklis2001regression}, we estimate at
every stop the expected cost of \emph{continuing optimally}, by backward
induction over historical paths with isotonic regression as the
conditional-expectation estimator, and hand off exactly when that estimate
exceeds the price of handing off now. State-dependent recourse prices ---
a handoff with twenty stops remaining costs more than one with two ---
enter the rule with no structural change, which a scalar threshold cannot
accommodate even in principle.

Our contributions are as follows. (i) We formalise the execution problem
as an optimal stopping problem on the running net-load process and
quantify the endpoint-label bias: the endpoint event is always a subset of
the peak event, and on collect-then-deliver structures the induced policy
degenerates to inaction with a provable cost inflation of a factor
$C_{\mathrm{fail}}/\omega_F$ over the preventive optimum
(Proposition~\ref{prop:bias}). (ii) We prove that the continuation value
is monotone in the running load under mild conditions
(Proposition~\ref{prop:monotone}), which justifies isotonic regression as
the estimator inside the backward induction, and that the classical
fixed-threshold rule is the myopic special case of our trigger and
over-triggers early in the route (Proposition~\ref{prop:myopic}). (iii) We
introduce a realistic last-mile cost model --- contracted fleet day rates,
standby handoffs priced on the remaining tour, surge-priced emergencies
with per-customer service compensation --- and show that the
Longstaff--Schwartz trigger absorbs it natively. (iv) We evaluate against
the endpoint-trained policy, its label-corrected threshold variant, and a
clairvoyant lower bound, on structural synthetic scenarios, on the 40
Dethloff benchmark instances \citep{dethloff2001vehicle} under three
planning regimes of increasing conservatism, and under the realistic cost
model, with paired significance tests throughout.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}

Stochastic vehicle routing has been studied since the observation of
\citet{dror1989vehicle} and \citet{bertsimas1992vehicle} that demand
uncertainty changes the structure of optimal routing decisions, with
recourse actions ranging from restocking trips to route resequencing; see
\citet{gendreau1996stochastic} and \citet{oyola2018stochastic} for
surveys. The simultaneous pickup-and-delivery variant originates with
\citet{min1989multiple} and was popularised by the benchmark instances of
\citet{dethloff2001vehicle}; modern heuristics for the deterministic
problem include cluster insertion \citep{salhi1999cluster}, particle swarm
optimisation \citep{ai2009particle}, parallel local search
\citep{subramanian2010parallel}, and adaptive large neighbourhood search
descending from \citet{ropke2006adaptive}. Our work is complementary to
this planning literature: we take the planned routes as given --- produced
here by an ALNS planner under deterministic, sample-average, and
Wasserstein distributionally robust \citep{esfahani2018data} feasibility
gates --- and optimise the \emph{execution} of each route under realised
uncertainty.

Execution-stage recourse connects to approximate dynamic programming for
routing, where rollout policies \citep{secomandi2001rollout}, lookup-table
value functions \citep{novoa2009approximate}, and offline--online hybrids
\citep{ulmer2019offline} approximate the cost-to-go of continuing a tour.
Our setting differs in that the per-route decision is a pure stopping
decision --- continue or transfer --- which admits the machinery of
optimal stopping \citep{chow1971great,peskir2006optimal} rather than a
general action space. We adopt the regression-based backward induction of
\citet{longstaff2001valuing} and \citet{tsitsiklis2001regression},
originally developed for American-option pricing, whose convergence was
analysed by \citet{clement2002analysis}. Our estimator choice exploits
problem structure: because the continuation cost is monotone in the
running load (Proposition~\ref{prop:monotone}), we use isotonic regression
\citep{robbins1955empirical,barlow1972statistical}, which is
tuning-parameter-free and shape-constrained, in place of the polynomial
bases common in finance.

\section{Problem formulation}\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Load dynamics and breach event}

Consider one planned route serving $m$ customers in fixed sequence
$1,\dots,m$ from a depot. The vehicle departs with the loaded deliveries;
at stop $k$ it delivers a random volume $d_k \ge 0$ and collects a random
pickup $p_k \ge 0$, producing the \emph{net increment}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:increment}
  g_k \;=\; p_k - d_k ,
\end{equation}
observed upon serving the stop. The \emph{running net load} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:runsum}
  W_k \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{k} g_j , \qquad W_0 = 0 ,
\end{equation}
and with departure load $L_0$ and capacity $Q$ the vehicle physically
overflows at stop $k$ when $L_0 + W_k > Q$, i.e.\ when $W_k$ exceeds the
\emph{slack} $B = Q - L_0$. Define the breach time
\begin{equation}\label{eq:breach}
  T \;=\; \min\{\,k \le m : W_k > B\,\}, \qquad
  \min\emptyset := \infty .
\end{equation}
The route \emph{peak-overflows} on the event $\{T \le m\} =
\{\max_{k\le m} W_k > B\}$. We write $\mathcal F_k =
\sigma(g_1,\dots,g_k)$ for the information available after serving stop
$k$.

\subsection{Decisions, prices, and objective}

After serving stop $k < m$ without a breach, the operator either
\emph{continues} or \emph{hands off}, transferring the remaining stops
$k{+}1,\dots,m$ to a standby vehicle at price $H_k > 0$. If the vehicle
continues and breaches at stop $j$, an emergency dispatch costs $E_j$;
if it completes all $m$ stops without breaching, execution costs nothing
beyond the planned travel. Prices are route-specific and known before
departure: in the realistic model of Section~\ref{sec:costs}, $H_k$
prices a standby callout plus the remaining tour distance at a mild
surcharge, and $E_j$ prices a surge callout, the remaining distance at a
surge multiplier, and service compensation for the $m-j$ downstream
customers, so both schedules decrease along the route and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:prices}
  E_k \;>\; H_k \quad\text{for all } k ,
\end{equation}
reflecting that an unplanned breach at a stop is always dearer than a
planned transfer at the same stop. The flat model $H_k \equiv \omega_F$,
$E_k \equiv C_{\mathrm{fail}}$ with $C_{\mathrm{fail}} > \omega_F$ is the
special case used by v1 and in our controlled experiments.

An \emph{execution policy} is a stopping time $\sigma$ with respect to
$(\mathcal F_k)$ taking values in $\{1,\dots,m-1\} \cup \{\infty\}$
($\sigma = \infty$ meaning never hand off), and the realised execution
cost is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cost}
  J(\sigma) \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    E_T, & T \le \sigma \wedge m \quad\text{(breach first)},\\[2pt]
    H_\sigma, & \sigma < T \quad\text{(handoff first)},\\[2pt]
    0, & \sigma = \infty,\; T = \infty
    \quad\text{(clean completion)} .
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The operator seeks $\inf_\sigma \mathbb E\, J(\sigma)$. Throughout we
assume the following.

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:markov}
The increments $g_1,\dots,g_m$ are independent (not necessarily
identically distributed), so that $(W_k)$ is Markov and $W_k$ is a
sufficient statistic for the future of the load path.
\end{assumption}

Assumption~\ref{ass:markov} matches the data-generating processes of both
our industrial motivation and the benchmark protocol (correlated
scenarios are examined empirically in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}; the
policy degrades gracefully because the conditioning statistic $W_k$
absorbs a common demand factor).

\subsection{Dynamic-programming characterisation}

By finite-horizon dynamic programming, define the value functions
backwards from $V_m(w) \equiv 0$ on $\{w \le B\}$ by
\begin{align}
  C_k(w) &\;=\;
  \mathbb E\!\left[\,
    E_{k+1}\,\mathbf 1\{W_{k+1} > B\}
    \;+\; V_{k+1}(W_{k+1})\,\mathbf 1\{W_{k+1} \le B\}
    \,\middle|\, W_k = w \right],
  \label{eq:contvalue}\\[3pt]
  V_k(w) &\;=\; \min\{\, H_k ,\; C_k(w) \,\}, \qquad k = m-1,\dots,1 .
  \label{eq:bellman}
\end{align}
$C_k(w)$ is the expected cost of \emph{continuing past stop $k$ and
playing optimally thereafter}, and the optimal policy is the first-passage
rule
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rule}
  \sigma^\star \;=\; \min\{\,k < m \;:\; C_k(W_k) > H_k \,\},
\end{equation}
by the standard verification argument for finite-horizon stopping
problems \citep{chow1971great,peskir2006optimal}. All the difficulty is
statistical: $C_k$ is unknown and must be estimated from historical load
paths.

\section{The OTR-2.0 policy}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Why the endpoint label fails}

The v1 system estimated $p_k(w) = \mathbb P(\text{overflow} \mid W_k = w)$
by isotonic regression of a binary label on $W_k$ across historical
routes, and triggered when $p_k(W_k) > \tau$. The label, however, was
$Y^{\mathrm{end}} = \mathbf 1\{W_m > B\}$ --- computed from the endpoint
of the path rather than from its running peak
$Y^{\mathrm{peak}} = \mathbf 1\{\max_{k \le m} W_k > B\}$. The following
elementary observation shows the defect is structural, not statistical,
and quantifies its cost; despite its simplicity we state it formally
because it explains a genuine field failure and because analogous
endpoint-for-peak substitutions appear whenever monitoring data are
aggregated per route.

\begin{proposition}[Endpoint-label bias]\label{prop:bias}
For every path law and every $B$,
$\{W_m > B\} \subseteq \{\max_{k\le m} W_k > B\}$, hence
$\mathbb P(Y^{\mathrm{end}}=1) \le \mathbb P(Y^{\mathrm{peak}}=1)$, and
the endpoint-trained regression estimates a conditional probability of
the \emph{wrong} event. The gap is extremal on collect-then-deliver
routes: if the route structure forces $W_m = 0$ almost surely (all
collected volume is delivered back) and $B > 0$, then
$Y^{\mathrm{end}} = 0$ a.s.\ while $\mathbb P(T \le m)$ may be arbitrary
in $[0,1)$. In that regime, under flat prices
$(\omega_F, C_{\mathrm{fail}})$, any threshold policy trained on
endpoint labels converges to the never-handoff policy with expected cost
$C_{\mathrm{fail}}\,\mathbb P(T\le m)$, whereas the stopping rule
\eqref{eq:rule} attains at most
$\omega_F\,\mathbb P(2 \le T \le m) + C_{\mathrm{fail}}\,
\mathbb P(T = 1)$. Consequently, whenever breaches occur but rarely at
the first stop, the endpoint-trained policy inflates execution cost by
approximately the factor $C_{\mathrm{fail}}/\omega_F$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The inclusion is immediate since $W_m \le \max_{k\le m} W_k$. If
$W_m = 0$ a.s.\ and $B > 0$ then $Y^{\mathrm{end}} = 0$ a.s., so any
regression of $Y^{\mathrm{end}}$ on any covariate is the zero function on
the support of the data; a trigger of the form
$\hat p_k(W_k) > \tau$ with $\tau > 0$ never fires, and the policy pays
$E_T = C_{\mathrm{fail}}$ exactly on $\{T \le m\}$. For the upper bound
on the optimum, consider the feasible (not necessarily optimal) rule
that hands off at the first $k$ with $\mathbb P(T = k+1 \mid
\mathcal F_k) = 1$; more simply, the clairvoyant-dominated bound follows
because rule \eqref{eq:rule} is optimal and therefore no worse than the
policy of Section~\ref{sec:oracle} restricted to information
$\mathcal F_k$: on $\{2 \le T \le m\}$ a handoff at $T-1$ costs
$\omega_F$, on $\{T = 1\}$ no decision epoch precedes the breach, and on
$\{T = \infty\}$ continuing forever costs $0$. Taking expectations gives
the stated bound, and the ratio of the two costs tends to
$C_{\mathrm{fail}}/\omega_F$ as $\mathbb P(T=1) \to 0$.
\end{proof}

OTR-2.0 therefore labels and calibrates on the running peak everywhere:
the empirical slack calibration solves
$\hat B = \inf\{b : \frac1N \sum_s \mathbf 1\{\max_k W^{(s)}_k > b\}
\le \alpha\}$, i.e.\ the $(1{-}\alpha)$-quantile of historical
\emph{peaks}, never of endpoint totals.

\subsection{Longstaff--Schwartz backward induction with isotonic
regression}\label{sec:lsm}

Estimating \eqref{eq:contvalue} requires the conditional expectation of
future costs \emph{under optimal future play}, which is exactly the
structure of American-option pricing by simulation. Following
\citet{longstaff2001valuing}, we sweep historical paths backwards,
maintaining for each path $s$ the realised future cost $\phi_s$ under the
policy already constructed for later stops (initialised by
$\phi_s = E_{T_s}$ if path $s$ breaches, else $0$), regress $\phi$ on
$W_k$ among the paths still alive at stop $k$ to obtain $\hat C_k$, and
then apply the implied action --- replace $\phi_s$ by $H_k$ on paths where
$\hat C_k(W_{k,s}) > H_k$ --- so that stop $k-1$ sees costs under the
improved later policy. The regressor is isotonic regression
\citep{barlow1972statistical}: the target is a cost supported on
$\{0\} \cup \{H_j\}_{j>k} \cup \{E_j\}_{j>k}$ rather than a probability,
but the shape constraint is justified by the following monotonicity, which
is the second structural pillar of the method.

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:prices}
$H_k \le E_k$ for all $k$, and both schedules are non-increasing in $k$.
\end{assumption}

Assumption~\ref{ass:prices} holds by construction in the realistic model
(both prices are affine in the remaining tour distance, and emergencies
additionally pay downstream compensation) and trivially in the flat
model.

\begin{proposition}[Monotone continuation value]\label{prop:monotone}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:markov} and \ref{ass:prices}, for every
$k \in \{1,\dots,m-1\}$ the continuation value $C_k(\cdot)$ of
\eqref{eq:contvalue} is non-decreasing on $(-\infty, B]$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Backward induction on $k$. Write the integrand of \eqref{eq:contvalue}
as $\varphi_{k+1}(x) = E_{k+1}\mathbf 1\{x > B\} +
V_{k+1}(x)\mathbf 1\{x \le B\}$ so that, using
Assumption~\ref{ass:markov},
$C_k(w) = \mathbb E\,\varphi_{k+1}(w + g_{k+1})$ with $g_{k+1}$
independent of $w$. It suffices that $\varphi_{k+1}$ be non-decreasing,
for then monotonicity is preserved by the shift $w \mapsto w + g_{k+1}$
pathwise and by expectation. At $k+1 = m$ we have $V_m \equiv 0 \le E_m$,
so $\varphi_m$ is a non-decreasing step function. For $k+1 < m$, by the
inductive hypothesis $C_{k+1}$ is non-decreasing, hence
$V_{k+1} = \min\{H_{k+1}, C_{k+1}\}$ is non-decreasing on
$(-\infty, B]$, and moreover $V_{k+1} \le H_{k+1} \le E_{k+1}$ by
Assumption~\ref{ass:prices}. Consider $x \le x'$. If both exceed $B$,
$\varphi_{k+1}(x) = \varphi_{k+1}(x') = E_{k+1}$. If both are at most
$B$, $\varphi_{k+1}(x) = V_{k+1}(x) \le V_{k+1}(x') =
\varphi_{k+1}(x')$. If $x \le B < x'$, then $\varphi_{k+1}(x) =
V_{k+1}(x) \le E_{k+1} = \varphi_{k+1}(x')$. Thus $\varphi_{k+1}$ is
non-decreasing in every case, completing the induction.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:monotone} makes isotonic regression the natural
estimator: it is the least-squares projection onto exactly the function
class known to contain the truth, requires no basis choice or bandwidth,
and inherits classical consistency guarantees
\citep{robbins1955empirical,barlow1972statistical}. The convergence of
the full backward-induction scheme to the optimal stopping value as the
number of historical paths grows follows the analysis of
\citet{clement2002analysis}, whose arguments require only that each
regression step be an $L^2$-consistent estimator of the conditional
expectation within the approximating class; monotone functions on a
compact range form a Glivenko--Cantelli class, so the isotonic step
satisfies this requirement whenever the truth is monotone, which
Proposition~\ref{prop:monotone} guarantees.

\begin{remark}
For small histories ($N \lesssim 500$ paths) the backward induction
amplifies estimation noise, because errors at late stops propagate into
the regression targets of early stops. OTR-2.0 then falls back to the
\emph{label-corrected threshold} variant: isotonic estimation of the
peak-overflow probability $p_k(w) = \mathbb P(T \le m \mid W_k = w,\,
T > k)$ and a single threshold tuned on realised training cost. The
fallback inherits the label fix but not the option-value correction; its
empirical price is quantified in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The online rule and its optimality for the fitted models}

Online, the vehicle updates $W_k$ at each stop, checks the physical guard
$W_k > B$ (which triggers the emergency price $E_k$ --- the guard always
ran on the running sum even in v1; the endpoint defect was in the
\emph{training label} only), and otherwise hands off exactly when
\begin{equation}\label{eq:trigger}
  \hat C_k(W_k) \;>\; H_k .
\end{equation}
No threshold exists to tune: \eqref{eq:trigger} compares two costs in the
same unit. Because $\hat C_k$ already prices every future opportunity to
hand off (that is precisely what the backward induction computed), rule
\eqref{eq:trigger} is the exact optimal-stopping rule for the fitted
models, not a heuristic approximation. The fixed-threshold rule of v1 is
recovered as its myopic degeneration, and the direction of its error is
one-sided:

\begin{proposition}[Myopic over-triggering]\label{prop:myopic}
Take flat prices $H_k \equiv \omega_F$, $E_k \equiv C_{\mathrm{fail}}$,
and let $p_k(w) = \mathbb P(T \le m \mid W_k = w, T > k)$ be the true
conditional peak-overflow probability. The v1-style myopic rule
``hand off iff $C_{\mathrm{fail}}\, p_k(w) > \omega_F$'', i.e.\
$p_k(w) > \tau$ with $\tau = \omega_F / C_{\mathrm{fail}}$, has stopping
region containing that of the optimal rule \eqref{eq:rule} at every
stop:
\[
  \{\, w : C_k(w) > \omega_F \,\} \;\subseteq\;
  \{\, w : C_{\mathrm{fail}}\, p_k(w) > \omega_F \,\} .
\]
The inclusion is strict at stop $k$ whenever
$\mathbb P(T > k+1 \mid W_k = w, T > k) > 0$ on a set of $w$ of positive
measure, i.e.\ whenever the breach, if it comes, may still be more than
one stop away.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
``Continue and never act again'' is a feasible continuation policy whose
expected cost from state $(k, w)$ is exactly
$C_{\mathrm{fail}}\, p_k(w)$; since $C_k(w)$ is the infimum over all
continuation policies, $C_k(w) \le C_{\mathrm{fail}}\, p_k(w)$, which
gives the inclusion. For strictness, split
$p_k(w) = q_1(w) + q_2(w)$ with
$q_1(w) = \mathbb P(T = k+1 \mid \cdot)$ and
$q_2(w) = \mathbb P(k+1 < T \le m \mid \cdot)$, and consider the
feasible policy ``continue one stop; if no breach, hand off at $k+1$ iff
$p_{k+1}(W_{k+1})\,C_{\mathrm{fail}} > \omega_F$, else continue and
never act''. Its cost is at most
$C_{\mathrm{fail}}\, q_1(w) + \omega_F\, q_2(w) +
0\cdot\mathbb P(T = \infty \mid \cdot)$
on the event that the one-stop-later probability exceeds the myopic
threshold, and in general at most
$C_{\mathrm{fail}}\,q_1(w) + \min\{\omega_F, C_{\mathrm{fail}}\,
\bar p\}\, q_2(w)$ where $\bar p \le 1$; whenever $q_2(w) > 0$ this is
strictly below $C_{\mathrm{fail}}(q_1(w) + q_2(w)) =
C_{\mathrm{fail}}\,p_k(w)$ because $\omega_F < C_{\mathrm{fail}}$.
Hence $C_k(w) < C_{\mathrm{fail}}\, p_k(w)$ there, and states with
$\omega_F$ between the two values are myopically stopped but optimally
continued.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:myopic} explains the second field observation:
tuned thresholds drifted upward (toward less triggering) precisely on
long routes, chasing the option value the functional form cannot
express. The state-dependent generalisation makes the failure
qualitative: with per-stop prices $H_k$, the myopic comparison
$p_k(w)\,E_{\text{(some)}} > H_k$ is not even well-posed as a single
threshold on $p_k$, whereas \eqref{eq:trigger} is unchanged.

\subsection{Clairvoyant lower bound}\label{sec:oracle}

To measure how much of the achievable saving each policy captures, we
report a perfect-information bound: a clairvoyant that sees the whole
path pays $0$ on $\{T = \infty\}$, and on $\{T = j \le m\}$ pays
$\min\{\min_{k < j} H_k,\; E_j\}$ --- the cheapest of any preemptive
handoff or, if all are dearer, the breach itself; a breach at the first
stop leaves no decision epoch. No policy adapted to
$(\mathcal F_k)$ can pay less on any path, so the bound is valid
pathwise, and it is attained by an $\mathcal F$-adapted policy only in
degenerate cases. The \emph{optimality gap} we report is the share of
the oracle-achievable saving a policy fails to capture, relative to the
never-handoff (reactive) baseline.

\subsection{Algorithm statement and complexity}

Algorithms~\ref{alg:offline} and \ref{alg:online} state the method;
Figure~\ref{fig:flow} shows the online loop.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{OTR-2.0 offline phase: peak-aware Longstaff--Schwartz fit}
\label{alg:offline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require historical increments $g^{(s)}_k$, $s = 1..N$, $k = 1..m$;
         slack $B$; price schedules $(H_k)$, $(E_k)$
\State $W^{(s)}_k \gets \sum_{j\le k} g^{(s)}_j$;\quad
       $T_s \gets \min\{k : W^{(s)}_k > B\}$ ($\infty$ if none)
       \Comment{peak, not endpoint}
\State $\phi_s \gets E_{T_s}\,\mathbf 1\{T_s \le m\}$
       \Comment{cost under ``never hand off''}
\For{$k = m-1$ \textbf{down to} $1$}
  \State $A \gets \{s : T_s > k\}$ \Comment{paths alive at stop $k$}
  \State $\hat C_k \gets$ isotonic fit of $\{\phi_s\}_{s\in A}$ on
         $\{W^{(s)}_k\}_{s\in A}$
  \State $\phi_s \gets H_k$ for all $s \in A$ with
         $\hat C_k(W^{(s)}_k) > H_k$
         \Comment{apply implied optimal action}
\EndFor
\State \Return $\{\hat C_k\}_{k=1}^{m-1}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{OTR-2.0 online phase: cost-comparison trigger}
\label{alg:online}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $m$, $B$, $(H_k)$, $(E_k)$, fitted $\{\hat C_k\}$
\State $W \gets 0$
\For{$k = 1$ \textbf{to} $m$}
  \State serve stop $k$; observe $d_k, p_k$;\quad $W \gets W + p_k - d_k$
  \If{$W > B$} \Return (\textsc{Emergency}, $E_k$) \EndIf
                \Comment{physical guard: running peak}
  \If{$k = m$} \Return (\textsc{Complete}, $0$) \EndIf
  \If{$\hat C_k(W) > H_k$} \Return (\textsc{Handoff}, $H_k$) \EndIf
      \Comment{no threshold $\tau$ exists}
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{A realistic last-mile cost model}\label{sec:costs}

Benchmark studies of stochastic routing conventionally price recourse
with two constants. Real operators face a richer bill, and because the
trigger \eqref{eq:trigger} compares costs directly, OTR-2.0 can consume
the richer bill without structural change. Guided by the economics of
Southeast-Asian last-mile platforms operating mixed contracted and
gig-economy fleets, we model, per operating day and per route:
\begin{align}
  \text{fixed:}\quad & F_{\mathrm{plan}} \cdot K
      \;+\; c_{\mathrm{km}} \cdot \mathrm{Travel},
  \label{eq:fixedcost}\\
  \text{handoff at } k:\quad
  H_k \;=\;& F_{\mathrm{ho}}
      + s_{\mathrm{ho}}\, c_{\mathrm{km}}\, R_k
      + c_{\mathrm{transfer}},
  \label{eq:handoffcost}\\
  \text{emergency at } k:\quad
  E_k \;=\;& F_{\mathrm{emg}}
      + s_{\mathrm{emg}}\, c_{\mathrm{km}}\, R_k
      + p_{\mathrm{late}} (m - k)
      + p_{\mathrm{breach}},
  \label{eq:emergencycost}
\end{align}
where $K$ is the number of planned vehicles, $R_k$ the tour distance
remaining after stop $k$, $F_{\mathrm{plan}}$ a contracted
vehicle-day rate, $c_{\mathrm{km}}$ the variable running cost,
$F_{\mathrm{ho}} < F_{\mathrm{emg}}$ callout fees for the standby pool
and the surge market respectively, $s_{\mathrm{ho}} < s_{\mathrm{emg}}$
the corresponding per-kilometre multipliers, $c_{\mathrm{transfer}}$ the
cargo-transfer dwell, $p_{\mathrm{late}}$ the service-level compensation
per downstream customer served late, and $p_{\mathrm{breach}}$ a churn
allowance for the customer at the breach point. Both schedules decrease
along the route and satisfy Assumption~\ref{ass:prices} by construction;
an early emergency is the worst outcome the model can produce, which
matches operational experience. Defaults
($F_{\mathrm{plan}}{=}35$, $c_{\mathrm{km}}{=}0.10$,
$F_{\mathrm{ho}}{=}12$, $s_{\mathrm{ho}}{=}1.2$,
$c_{\mathrm{transfer}}{=}3$, $F_{\mathrm{emg}}{=}40$,
$s_{\mathrm{emg}}{=}2.5$, $p_{\mathrm{late}}{=}1.5$,
$p_{\mathrm{breach}}{=}10$, in currency units per day) are
order-of-magnitude calibrated to public gig-logistics figures and are
swept in the sensitivity study. Under this model the total budget cost of
a plan is \eqref{eq:fixedcost} plus the expected execution cost of its
routes, and execution policies act on the latter alone.

\section{Experimental design}\label{sec:experiments}

Our evaluation has three layers, all with train/test scenario
separation, and all comparing the same six execution policies:
\textsc{none} (reactive: never hand off), \textsc{v1} with endpoint
labels and a cost-tuned threshold, the myopic threshold, the
label-corrected threshold fallback (\textsc{fb}), \textsc{OTR-2.0}
(\textsc{v2}), and the clairvoyant oracle of Section~\ref{sec:oracle}.

\paragraph{Structural synthetic scenarios.} Following the diagnosis of
Proposition~\ref{prop:bias} we generate (i) \emph{collect-then-deliver}
routes whose endpoint is identically zero while the peak varies, (ii)
\emph{milk-run} routes with a rising net-load ramp and a two-regime
day-volume mixture, and (iii) the milk run under a high emergency-price
ratio $C_{\mathrm{fail}}/\omega_F = 20$; five seeds, $10^4$ training and
$1.2\times 10^4$ test routes per seed, $m = 12$, slack calibrated to a
10\% peak-overflow rate.

\paragraph{Benchmark instances.} The 40 Dethloff instances
\citep{dethloff2001vehicle} (CON and SCA families, 50 customers each) are
solved by an ALNS planner under three feasibility gates of increasing
conservatism --- deterministic (\textsc{Det}), sample-average CVaR
(\textsc{SAA}), and Wasserstein-robust (\textsc{WDRO})
\citep{esfahani2018data} --- and every route of every plan is then
executed under all policies with $10^3$ training and $2\times 10^3$ test
scenarios per route (gamma marginals, coefficient of variation $0.3$,
equicorrelated Gaussian copula $\rho = 0.6$, the protocol of the
underlying planning study). This yields 120 plan-level paired
comparisons.

\paragraph{Realistic-cost evaluation and sensitivity.} The same
instances and plans are re-executed under the cost model of
Section~\ref{sec:costs}, with per-stop schedules built from the true
route geometry. The sensitivity study sweeps the emergency-price ratio,
history size $N$, route length $m$, increment family (gamma, lognormal,
Student-$t$), and the strength of a common demand factor, reporting
paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests of \textsc{v2} against the strongest
tuned competitor in every cell.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We studied the execution stage of stochastic routing with simultaneous
pickup and delivery as a finite-horizon optimal stopping problem, traced
a real field failure to an endpoint-for-peak label substitution whose
bias we quantified, and proposed OTR-2.0: peak-aware labels combined
with a Longstaff--Schwartz backward induction whose isotonic regressions
are justified by a monotone continuation value. The resulting trigger
compares estimated continuation cost against the state-dependent price
of acting now, eliminates threshold tuning, provably never triggers
later than optimal play would while avoiding the myopic rule's premature
handoffs, and absorbs realistic operator economics --- contracted
fleets, standby pools, surge-priced emergencies, service-level
compensation --- without structural change.

Limitations delineate future work. The conditioning statistic is the
scalar running sum; enriching it with remaining-route covariates gave no
measurable gain in our tests but may matter for heterogeneous
territories, and the isotonic step can be swapped for any monotone
learner without touching the induction. The handoff is all-or-nothing;
partial handoffs that transfer only the riskiest remaining customers
compose cleanly with the same continuation machinery but require
per-customer cost attribution. Increment independence
(Assumption~\ref{ass:markov}) is used by the theory though not by the
algorithm; our correlation sweep shows graceful degradation, and an
explicit common-factor state would be the disciplined extension.
Finally, planning and execution are optimised separately here; feeding
the fitted execution cost of a candidate route back into the planner's
acceptance gate --- so that routes are shaped to be cheap to execute,
not merely feasible --- is, we believe, the most promising direction
this line of work opens.
